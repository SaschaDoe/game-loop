# Lesser Tales of Aethermoor — Volume III

## Overview

The deepest secrets are the ones hiding in plain sight. A children's rhyme sung in every schoolyard. A library that rearranges its own shelves. A child's drawing that makes temple priests go pale. These stories don't announce themselves as important — they sit quietly in the world, waiting for someone to notice that something is wrong. Or right. Or both.

## 1. The Clockwork Bird of Maren Goss

**Type:** Engineering legend / philosophical mystery
**Region:** The Thornlands, Gearfall district
**Era:** Year ~1,200 After Ascension (creation); present (still flying)

### The Story

Maren Goss was a Clockwork Creed artificer — not a genius, she would have insisted, but a "competent mechanic with an unreasonable amount of patience." She spent her career building useful things: water pumps, grain mills, door locks, clockwork prosthetics for injured miners. Practical. Mundane. Deliberately, almost aggressively, non-magical.

The Clockwork Creed's central belief is that anything magic can do, engineering can do without divine dependency. Most Creed members focus on replacing magical conveniences — heating runes replaced by insulation, light crystals replaced by oil lamps, healing magic supplemented by surgery. Maren took this philosophy further than most.

She wanted to build something that flew.

Not glided — *flew*. Sustained, powered, controlled flight without a single rune, enchantment, or prayer. The Creed's engineers had been attempting mechanical flight for centuries. Every attempt failed. Wings broke. Engines were too heavy. Control surfaces couldn't adapt to wind.

Maren worked for twenty-two years.

Her workshop — a converted barn on the edge of Gearfall — became a graveyard of prototypes. Wooden frames. Articulated wings. Spring-driven mechanisms. Pressurized air systems. Dozens of approaches, each failing for a different reason. Her colleagues thought she was wasting her life. Her family thought she was mad. The town thought she was harmless but eccentric.

On a Tuesday morning in autumn — she never recorded the exact date, because she didn't realize it was significant until later — Maren wound the mainspring of Prototype 37, held the brass-and-copper bird in her open palm, and let go.

It flew.

Not well. Not gracefully. It stuttered into the air like a drunk starling, wings beating in a rhythm that seemed mechanically impossible given the spring's power output. But it flew — up, over the barn, past the treeline, into the open sky.

And it did not come down.

### Three Hundred Years Later

The clockwork bird is still flying. Three centuries of sustained mechanical flight, without maintenance, without fuel, without any power source that anyone can identify. The mainspring should have unwound in forty minutes. The copper should have corroded in a decade. The joints should have seized, the gears should have worn, the aerodynamics — which Maren herself admitted were "not quite right" — should not have produced sustained lift.

It circles. Always the same area — a patch of sky above an unremarkable hill three miles northeast of Gearfall. A wide, lazy circle, approximately one mile in diameter, repeated endlessly. Day and night. In storms and calms. Through winters that ground every living bird.

People have tried to catch it. Archers have shot at it (the arrows pass through its flight path moments after it moves — its timing is uncanny). Mages have attempted summoning spells (they fail — the bird is not magical, so there is nothing for the spell to grab). A Runeweaver built a net launcher capable of reaching its altitude. The bird dodged it with a lateral maneuver that should have been impossible for a spring-driven mechanism.

Maren Goss herself, in the final entry of her workshop journal (written the evening after the bird took flight), recorded: "It works. I don't understand why it works. The power calculations don't add up. The spring should be spent. I think something is moving the bird that isn't the spring. I built a machine, and something else is *using* it."

She died in her sleep a year later, at age seventy-four. She seemed at peace.

### What the Ashen Circle Suspects

The hill beneath the bird's circle is the surface expression of a buried Ley Line nexus — a minor convergence of the Order and Energy streams. The nexus is too small to attract attention from mages or temple surveyors, but it hums with a faint, steady resonance.

The bird is not magical. Maren built it without a single enchantment. But she built it with the precision of someone who spent twenty-two years obsessing over mechanical perfection — and perfection, in Aethermoor, is the province of Order. Aum-Varek's stream. The principle that holds things together, that enforces patterns, that sustains structure.

The bird doesn't fly because of its spring. It flies because its structure — the exact proportions of its wings, the precise frequency of its wingbeat, the specific resonance of copper-on-brass — accidentally matches the harmonic signature of the Ley Line beneath it. The nexus is *sustaining* the bird the way a river sustains a water wheel. Not consciously. Not intentionally. The bird simply fell into resonance, and the Ley Line keeps it there.

The cruelest irony: Maren Goss spent her life proving that engineering doesn't need magic. Her greatest creation — the proof of her life's philosophy — works because of magic. But it's not the Ascended's magic. It's Old Magic. The Ley Line's natural resonance, predating any god, responding to mechanical perfection the way it responds to anything that achieves harmony with the world's fundamental structure.

The Clockwork Creed doesn't need the gods. Maren was right about that. What she didn't know — what the bird proves — is that the world's magic doesn't need the gods either.

### Player Connection

- The bird is visible during Thornlands exploration as a glinting speck in the sky
- Investigating triggers a quest involving Maren's workshop journal and the buried nexus
- The Iron Remnant and the Clockwork Creed both want the bird studied — their conclusions differ on what it means
- A Primordialist who examines the nexus can confirm the Old Magic resonance — a lore revelation about the nature of the Ley Lines

## 2. The Inquisitor Who Read Too Much

**Type:** Character study / quiet tragedy
**Region:** Holy Empire of Solanthis
**Era:** Years 1,380–1,420 After Ascension

### The Story

Daveth Stern served the Inquisition for thirty-one years. He was good at his work — not brilliant, not famous, but thorough and reliable. He investigated reports of heretical texts, confiscated forbidden materials, and wrote meticulous reports. He was promoted twice, commended four times, and never once questioned whether his work was righteous.

His specialty was texts. While other Inquisitors hunted practitioners and infiltrated covens, Daveth hunted books. He had a gift for finding hidden manuscripts — a patience for searching that his colleagues lacked. Over three decades, he confiscated and destroyed over two thousand texts deemed heretical by the Church of the Radiant Sun.

The process was always the same: receive a report, investigate, confiscate, catalogue, destroy. The catalogue was important — the Inquisition kept records of what it burned, so that if a text resurfaced, they would recognize it. Daveth wrote each catalogue entry personally, summarizing the text's contents in a single paragraph before consigning it to the flames.

He never read the texts fully. That was procedure. "Read only enough to classify. Full engagement with heretical material risks contamination of the faithful mind." Daveth followed this rule for thirty-one years.

When he retired, at age sixty-three, the Inquisition gave him a commendation and a pension. He moved to a cottage in the countryside. He grew vegetables. He attended temple services every Solday. He was content.

Then, one winter evening, he began to think about the catalogue.

### The Reading

Daveth had kept personal copies of his catalogue entries — technically against regulation, but a minor infraction that no one had ever enforced. Two thousand texts, summarized in one paragraph each. He had written these summaries himself, decades ago, without paying attention to their content beyond classification.

He read them now.

Not quickly. Methodically, the way he did everything. One entry per evening, by candlelight, with a cup of tea. He read the summaries of texts he had burned about pre-Ascension history. About Ley Line mathematics that contradicted temple cosmology. About the Crystalborn civilization, which official history dismissed as minor. About the Iron Republics, which official history barely mentioned. About the thrones — described in a text he'd burned in Year 1,395 as "structural features of the world's magical architecture, predating any occupant."

He had written that summary himself. He had summarized the heresy, classified it, and burned the text without ever absorbing what the words *meant*.

Now, in retirement, reading his own handwriting by candlelight, the meaning arrived.

His journal from this period survives. The entries are clinical at first — the habit of an Inquisitor who writes reports, not feelings. But they change:

*Week 3: "The texts I burned in the Mirrow region described a war manufactured by forged documents. I classified this as 'historical revisionism.' Reclassification: it may be accurate. Cross-referencing four independent confiscations from different years, different authors, different regions — all describe the same forgeries. Coincidence requires more faith than heresy."*

*Week 8: "I have now identified seventeen texts — confiscated over a twenty-year period, from seventeen different sources, in seven different regions — that independently reference the existence of the thrones before the Ascended. I burned them all. Each one I classified as 'cosmological heresy.' I did not notice the pattern because I was not looking for patterns. I was looking for deviations from orthodoxy. It did not occur to me that orthodoxy might be the deviation."*

*Week 14: "I cannot sleep. I keep counting. Thirty-one years. Two thousand texts. How many of them were true? If even ten percent were genuine — if I burned two hundred truths — then I have not served justice. I have served its opposite."*

*Week 20: "I attended temple today. The priest read from the Scripture of the Radiant Dawn — the passage about Khorvan's charge against the Void Serpent. I have read, in texts I burned, six independent accounts from pre-Ascension sources that describe a man named Khorvan who fled every battle he was ever assigned. I sat in the pew and listened to the priest describe Khorvan's bravery and I felt physically ill."*

### What He Did

Daveth did not go public. He did not contact the Unmaskers. He did not write a manifesto or nail a treatise to a temple door. He was an Inquisitor, not a revolutionary. He understood, with the cold clarity of a man who had spent thirty-one years inside the system, that one retired Inquisitor's crisis of faith would change nothing.

Instead, he did something quieter.

He rewrote his catalogue.

Over the last two years of his life, Daveth reconstructed the contents of every text he could remember — not summaries, but the substance. Arguments, evidence, citations, conclusions. His memory was extraordinary (the Inquisition had trained it) and his recall of confiscated texts was near-perfect. He could not reproduce them word for word, but he could reconstruct their reasoning.

He filled eleven notebooks. The notebooks contain the distilled arguments of two thousand heretical texts — the collective case against the official mythology, assembled by a man who had spent his life trying to destroy it.

He hid the notebooks in a place only an Inquisitor would think to look: inside the walls of his cottage, behind the plasterwork, in a space designed to precisely mimic the hidden compartments he had spent thirty-one years finding and emptying.

He died in his sleep in Year 1,420, at age sixty-seven. His cottage was inherited by a niece who uses it as a summer home. The notebooks have not been found.

### Player Connection

- The cottage can be discovered during Holy Empire exploration
- Investigating the cottage requires the player to "think like an Inquisitor" — a puzzle mechanic involving hidden compartments
- The eleven notebooks are one of the most significant lore caches in the game — a systematic index of suppressed knowledge
- The Ashen Circle, the Unmaskers, and the Veiled Hand all have reason to want the notebooks
- Daveth's ghost does not haunt the cottage — he is at rest. But his journal (left openly on a shelf, as if inviting discovery) provides the emotional context for the notebooks' contents

## 3. The Counting Rhyme

**Type:** Children's folklore / hidden prophecy
**Region:** Continental — sung everywhere, with regional variations
**Era:** Unknown origin; documented for at least 800 years

### The Story

Every child in Aethermoor knows the Counting Rhyme. It's a clapping game, a jump-rope song, a lullaby, a nonsense verse — the kind of thing children sing without thinking, the way they breathe without thinking. It has regional variations (the Thornlands version is faster; the Solanthis version has an extra verse; the Ashlands version is sung in a minor key), but the core is always the same:

*One for the liar who speaks only truth,*
*Two for the thief who stole all of youth,*
*Three for the coward who charges the line,*
*Four for the torturer, gentle and kind,*
*Five for the poisoner, green as the spring,*
*Six for the fool who knows everything,*
*Seven for the judge who condemned the just —*
*Count them on fingers and look up from dust.*

Children sing it while counting fingers. They sing it while skipping rope. They sing it to tease each other ("you're number six!"). They have no idea what it means. Adults who hear it think nothing of it — it's a nonsense rhyme, like "ring around the rosies" or "one-two, buckle my shoe."

It is not nonsense.

### The Verses

Each line describes an Ascended — not by divine name but by the ironic contradiction between what they were and what they became:

1. **"The liar who speaks only truth"** — Theron, God of Truth, the greatest liar in history, now unable to lie
2. **"The thief who stole all of youth"** — Aelith, Goddess of Love, who stole her children's futures, now the embodiment of the nurturing she never gave
3. **"The coward who charges the line"** — Khorvan, God of War, who fled every battle, now the incarnation of martial fury
4. **"The torturer, gentle and kind"** — Mireya, Goddess of Mercy, the chief torturer, now compulsively healing
5. **"The poisoner, green as the spring"** — Selvara, Goddess of Nature, who poisoned the Verdant Basin, now dripping with life
6. **"The fool who knows everything"** — Orinthas, God of Wisdom, the catastrophically stupid king, now all-knowing
7. **"The judge who condemned the just"** — Verath, God of Justice, the corrupt magistrate who sentenced innocents, now compulsively fair

The seventh line is the most carefully hidden. If the rhyme was indeed composed by Sera Thornhaven's descendants (as the Ashen Circle theorizes), it makes sense that Verath's line would be the climax — his crime was the one that birthed the Unmaskers.

The final line — "Count them on fingers and look up from dust" — is interpreted by children as a literal instruction (count on your fingers, look up from the ground). But in Primordialist tradition, "looking up from dust" means "seeing past the surface of the world" — looking beyond the mundane to the truth beneath.

### Where It Came From

No one knows. The rhyme appears in written sources dating back at least eight centuries, but it was clearly oral tradition long before anyone wrote it down. It exists in every region, in every language, with the same core meaning despite surface variations. This kind of continental distribution usually indicates either a very old origin or an organized campaign of dissemination.

The Ashen Circle's best theory: the rhyme was composed by the Unmaskers — possibly by Sera Thornhaven's descendants in the early centuries after the Ascension — and deliberately seeded into children's oral culture as a way of preserving the truth in a form the Veiled Hand wouldn't think to suppress. And they were right. The Veiled Hand burns books, silences scholars, and assassinates prophets. It does not police nursery rhymes.

A competing theory (held by the Primordialists) is older and stranger: the rhyme was not composed by anyone. It emerged, the way folk songs emerge — spontaneously, from the collective unconscious, shaped by forces beneath conscious thought. The Ley Lines carry truth in their current. The truth seeps into dreams. Dreams become songs. Songs become nursery rhymes. The world is trying to tell its children what the gods won't.

### What the Veiled Hand Knows

The Veiled Hand has, in fact, noticed the Counting Rhyme. A junior analyst flagged it in Year 1,350 in a report titled "Potential Encoded Theological Content in Juvenile Oral Traditions." The report was filed, marked "low priority," and forgotten.

It resurfaced in Year 1,400 when a senior Finger (the Veiled Hand's highest rank) heard her own granddaughter singing it at a family dinner. She went very still. She excused herself. She retrieved the 1,350 report.

The Finger's response, appended to the report, reads: "We cannot suppress this. It is in every child's mouth on the continent. Attempting to ban a nursery rhyme would draw more attention than the rhyme itself. Recommend continued monitoring but no action. The truth has hidden itself in the one place we cannot reach — the space between a child's laughter and a grandmother's memory. Well played."

The report was re-filed under "Acknowledged Threats, Unresolvable."

### Player Connection

- The rhyme is heard throughout the game — children singing it in towns, NPCs humming it absently
- With enough lore knowledge, the player can decode the verses and match them to the Ascended
- Decoding the rhyme is not a quest — it's an organic "aha" moment for players paying attention
- The Ashen Circle offers a follow-up discussion if the player brings the decoded rhyme to them
- The seventh line ("the hollow who learned how to love") becomes significant during the endgame, when the player must decide whether the Ascended's transformations were genuine redemption or cosmic punishment

## 4. The Five-Minute Prophet

**Type:** Character study / chronomancy tale
**Region:** The Verdant Reach, the village of Millhaven
**Era:** Present day

### The Story

Old Dessa has lived in Millhaven for as long as anyone remembers. She keeps a garden, mends clothes for a few coins, and plays cards at the Rooster's Crow tavern every Tenthday evening. She wins more often than chance allows — not every game, not suspiciously often, but enough that the regulars have learned to watch their tells.

She is, by all outward appearances, a perfectly ordinary elderly woman.

She catches falling things.

Not dramatically — not diving across rooms or lunging heroically. She simply... adjusts. A child trips near her, and Dessa's hand is already there, catching an elbow before the knee hits cobblestone. A merchant fumbles a crate of apples, and Dessa is holding the bottom before the wood gives way. A gust of wind takes a hat, and Dessa has already turned to face the direction the hat will go.

The villagers call it "Dessa's luck." They say she has quick reflexes. They say she pays attention. They don't think about it much, because Dessa doesn't make it dramatic. She just... knows. Five minutes before it happens.

### What Dessa Is

Dessa is a Chronomancer — the last practitioner of the Ash-Maren lineage, the most disciplined school of temporal magic.

Chronomancy — the manipulation of temporal Ley Lines — was forbidden by the Ascended centuries ago. The official reason: Tho-Rienne's Ley Lines are the most damaged, and every use of chronomancy risks widening the cracks. The real reason: time magic reveals historical truths the Ascended want buried.

The Inquisition hunted Chronomancers with particular zeal. By Year 1,000, the Ash-Maren school — the tradition's most formalized lineage — was effectively extinct. The last known master — a woman named Kaelin Ash-Maren — was killed during a raid in Year 1,003. Or so the Inquisition believed. (Other chronomantic practices may survive in fragmentary form among Arcanists and Primordialists, but none with the Ash-Maren discipline.)

Kaelin survived. She fled to the Verdant Reach, changed her name, and stopped practicing — almost entirely. She allowed herself one indulgence: a five-minute window. Just enough temporal sight to live comfortably but not enough to attract attention. She taught the technique to her daughter. Her daughter taught her daughter. Four generations of women, each maintaining the smallest possible chronomantic practice, each living quiet lives in quiet villages.

Dessa is the fifth generation.

Her window is five minutes. She can see, with crystalline clarity, everything that will happen in the next five minutes. Beyond that, the future is fog. She uses this gift for small things — catching plates, avoiding puddles, winning card games, knowing when to bring an umbrella. It is the most modest application of temporal magic in history.

### The Exception

Twice in her life, Dessa's window has *stretched*.

The first time was when she was twelve. She was sitting by the river, skipping stones, and suddenly she saw — not five minutes ahead but five *centuries*. She saw a figure standing on a Ley Line nexus, surrounded by seven streams of light converging into one. The figure was singing. The ground was shaking. Seven shapes — vast, divine, terrified — were leaning away from the light as if it burned them.

She saw the Singer.

The vision lasted three seconds and left her with a nosebleed and a headache that lasted a week. She told no one. She did not understand what she had seen.

The second time was last year. She was in her garden, pulling turnips, and the window opened again — five centuries, maybe more. She saw the same nexus, the same convergence. But this time, instead of one outcome, she saw *three* — overlapping, simultaneous, contradictory. In one, the Singer shattered the masks and the world was flooded with light. In another, the Singer shattered the masks and the world was flooded with *darkness*. In the third, the Singer did not shatter anything — the Singer *put the masks back on*, gently, and the world continued as it was, and the gods wept with relief.

Dessa pulled her turnips. She went inside. She made tea. She has not spoken of it.

When asked by the regulars at the Rooster's Crow if anything is wrong (she has been quieter than usual), she says: "I'm just thinking about endings. There's always more than one, and you never know which one you'll get until you get it."

They assume she's talking about card games.

### Player Connection

- Dessa is encountered during Verdant Reach exploration
- She functions as a card game opponent (a mini-game) and a general merchant
- Players with high Chronomancy or Time attunement notice something unusual about her — she reacts to things before they happen
- If the player earns her trust (a series of small kindness-based interactions), she reveals her nature
- She can teach the player a unique Chronomancy technique: the Five-Minute Window (a gameplay ability that provides short-range future sight)
- She will not discuss what she saw in her long visions, but her reaction to certain endgame-related topics hints that she has glimpsed the possible endings

## 5. The Map That Lied to Save the World

**Type:** Spy story / cartographic mystery
**Region:** Multiple — the map covers the continent
**Era:** Years 1,250–1,290 After Ascension

### The Story

Olis Dray was the best cartographer the Veiled Hand ever employed. Not a field agent — he was too old for that, too slow, too nearsighted. But give him survey data, expedition reports, and a good set of inks, and he could produce maps of astonishing accuracy. The Veiled Hand used him for their most sensitive project: the Ruin Registry.

The Ruin Registry was a comprehensive map of every known pre-Ascension site in the world — ruins, buried cities, cave systems, sunken structures, temporal anomalies. The Veiled Hand had been compiling data for centuries, but no one had ever assembled it into a single document. The project was Olis's life's work: a master map showing every location where dangerous pre-Ascension evidence might survive.

The purpose of the map was destruction. Once complete, the Veiled Hand would systematically visit every site and eliminate anything that contradicted the official mythology. The Ruin Registry would be the blueprint for the final erasure of the Age of Silence.

Olis began the work in Year 1,250. He was given access to four centuries of Veiled Hand survey data, including reports from embedded agents in the Ashen Circle. He cross-referenced, compiled, verified. He was meticulous.

He was also, slowly, being changed by what he mapped.

### The Change

Olis had never seen the ruins himself. He was a desk cartographer — his world was parchment and ink. But as he plotted location after location, pattern after pattern, the shape of the pre-Ascension world emerged on his desk like a photograph developing.

He began to see what the Ley Line researchers had seen: the ruins weren't random. They formed networks. Trade routes. Communication lines. Cultural corridors. The pre-Ascension world had been *organized* — not by gods, but by people. Civilizations that built roads and traded goods and shared knowledge, centuries before the Ascended claimed to have created everything.

In Year 1,260, Olis began to make mistakes. A ruin's coordinates shifted by a fraction of a degree — enough to send a Veiled Hand team to the wrong valley. A site's classification changed from "confirmed" to "unverified" — enough to push it down the priority list indefinitely. A particularly significant location simply... disappeared from the Registry. Olis blamed transcription errors. His supervisors, who were not cartographers, accepted the explanations.

Over thirty years, Olis Dray systematically sabotaged his own map. He did not remove all the ruins — that would have been noticed. He protected perhaps one in ten. The ruins he saved were chosen with care: sites of particular beauty, sites containing irreplaceable knowledge, sites where his own mapping had revealed something too significant to destroy.

He kept a second map — the true map — hidden in a false bottom in his desk. This map showed everything: the real locations, the accurate classifications, the complete network of pre-Ascension civilization. It was the most accurate map of the ancient world ever created.

### What Happened

In Year 1,290, Olis retired. The Ruin Registry was declared complete. Veiled Hand teams spent the next century systematically visiting the sites it identified. They found and destroyed hundreds of pre-Ascension artifacts, inscriptions, and structures.

They also found, in roughly one case out of ten, that the coordinates led them to empty fields, wrong valleys, or sites that contained nothing of significance. These failures were attributed to the inherent difficulty of locating millennia-old ruins and to "natural degradation of site integrity."

Olis died in Year 1,295, at age seventy-eight. His true map — the accurate one — was bequeathed in a sealed box to a niece who, unbeknownst to the Veiled Hand, was a junior member of the Ashen Circle. She recognized what she held immediately.

The true map is now the Ashen Circle's most valuable document. It shows the locations of pre-Ascension sites that the Veiled Hand believes no longer exist — sites protected by a quiet old cartographer who decided, somewhere around his tenth year of mapping the world the Ascended destroyed, that some things are worth lying to protect.

### Player Connection

- The Ashen Circle questline eventually grants the player access to Olis's true map
- The map reveals hidden ruin locations across the continent — exploration sites containing significant lore artifacts
- Several of these ruins contain materials that the Veiled Hand destroyed at every other site — making them uniquely valuable
- Olis's story is told through his marginalia — notes in the map's margins that document his gradual transformation from loyal agent to quiet saboteur

## 6. The Ashwolf Vigil

**Type:** Animal story / ghost tale
**Region:** The Ashlands Territories, the Fracture War battlefields
**Era:** Year ~830 After Ascension to present

### The Story

In the southern Ashlands, where the scrubland gives way to the volcanic flats, there is a cairn of black stones. The stones are arranged carefully — not thrown in a pile but stacked, each one balanced, the way someone would build a cairn if they had no hands and had to use their mouth.

Beside the cairn lies an ashwolf.

It is the oldest ashwolf anyone has ever seen. Its fur, which should smolder with ember-bright tips, has cooled to a deep, steady red — the color of banked coals rather than living fire. Its eyes glow with a light that has dimmed from orange to deep amber. It is enormous — larger than any modern ashwolf, a relic of a time when the packs ran bigger.

It does not move. It lies beside the cairn, its great head resting on its paws, its eyes fixed on the southern horizon. It breathes. It blinks. Occasionally, it sighs — a sound like wind through cooling lava.

When travelers pass, it raises its head and watches. If they approach the cairn, it growls — a low, sustained rumble that vibrates in the chest. It has never bitten anyone. It has never needed to. The growl is sufficient.

If travelers leave the cairn alone and pass at a respectful distance, the ashwolf watches them go. Then it lowers its head back to its paws. And waits.

### What the Ashlands Nomads Know

The nomads call the ashwolf *Kel-Thura* — "the one who stays." They say it has been lying beside the cairn since their great-great-grandmothers were children. They leave offerings — dried meat, water in stone bowls — at the edge of the ashwolf's territory. The offerings are never touched. *Kel-Thura* does not eat. It has not eaten in living memory. The residual energy of Ira-Sethi, the Original Seven's Principle of Energy, sustains it the way it sustains all ashwolves — but *Kel-Thura* draws more deeply than any other. It pulls energy from the volcanic earth beneath it, from the residual heat of the Ashlands themselves. It feeds on the embers of a god who became the earth.

The nomads tell a story:

"During the Fracture War, when the temple soldiers came to burn the Unmaskers' hiding places, a soldier lost his way in the Ashlands. He was young. He was afraid. He had been ordered to burn villages, and he had done it, and now he was alone in the volcanic flats with the smoke of what he'd done still in his clothes.

"He deserted. He walked south, away from the war, into the deep Ashlands where no one goes. He intended to disappear. To become no one. To let the desert take him.

"But he was followed. An ashwolf pup — too young to be away from the pack, too stubborn to stay — padded after him through the black stone and the hot sand. The soldier tried to shoo it away. It came back. He threw stones near it. It dodged them and came back. He walked faster. The pup kept pace.

"In the end, the soldier sat down. The pup climbed into his lap. And the soldier — who had burned villages and hated himself for it — wept into the pup's fur while it licked the soot from his face.

"They lived together for three years, the deserter and the ashwolf. They walked the deep Ashlands. They ate what they could find. The soldier spoke to the wolf and the wolf listened, and perhaps that was enough.

"The soldier died. Not violently — his body simply wore out. The Ashlands are not kind to human bodies. He sat down one evening and did not stand up.

"The ashwolf did not leave.

"It has not left since."

### The Truth Beneath the Story

The nomads' story is substantially accurate. The soldier's bones are beneath the cairn — the ashwolf built it itself, stone by stone, over years. The soldier's identity is unknown. No military record matches a Fracture War deserter in the deep Ashlands.

But the ashwolf knows his name. And the ashwolf is waiting.

Primordialists who have approached *Kel-Thura* with Old Magic attunement report sensing a *purpose* in the creature — not the dumb persistence of an animal but the deliberate patience of a vigil. The ashwolf is waiting for the soldier's ghost. Not his lingering spirit (the soldier passed through the Spirit Membrane normally, as all dead do) but something else — a *promise*. The soldier, in his final days, made a statement that *Kel-Thura* heard and understood and took as a vow: "I'll come back. When I've figured out how to undo what I've done — when I know how to make it right — I'll come back for you."

*Kel-Thura* is a simple creature. It does not understand abstract concepts like redemption or impossibility. It understood only this: he said he would come back. So it waits.

The Ashlands are patient. So is the wolf. So, somewhere beyond the Spirit Membrane, is the soldier — who meant what he said, even if he didn't know how to keep the promise.

### Player Connection

- *Kel-Thura* is encountered during Ashlands exploration
- Approaching the cairn triggers the growl; retreating and showing respect allows interaction
- A player with Spirit magic can sense the soldier's promise resonating in the ashwolf's consciousness
- A high-level Spirit quest allows the player to reach the soldier's ghost and deliver a message — or bring the ghost back to keep his promise
- If reunited, the soldier's ghost and the ashwolf walk together into the deep Ashlands and are not seen again. The cairn remains, now tended by nomads as a shrine to loyalty

## 7. The Library That Has Opinions

**Type:** Domestic mystery / dark comedy
**Region:** Free Cities of the Coast, the town of Brackenmere
**Era:** Year ~1,100 After Ascension to present

### The Story

The Brackenmere Public Library is not impressive. It occupies a converted fisherman's warehouse — stone walls, low ceiling, a persistent smell of salt that no amount of lavender can mask. Its collection is modest: three hundred and twelve books, mostly donated, covering topics from animal husbandry to Zhaltish poetry. It has a single librarian — currently a woman named Pella, who inherited the position from her mother, who inherited it from *her* mother.

The library has one unusual feature: it sorts itself.

Every morning, Pella arrives to find the books in a different arrangement than the one she left. Not randomly rearranged — *categorized*. But not by the standard systems (author, title, subject). The library sorts by truthfulness.

Books that contain accurate information migrate to the front shelves, near the windows, where the light is best. Books that contain errors drift toward the middle. Books that contain deliberate lies — propaganda, falsified histories, invented genealogies — end up in the back corner, behind a shelf so warped by salt damage that patrons rarely venture there.

The most dramatic case: the temple-approved *Scripture of the Radiant Dawn*, donated by the local priest with great ceremony, was found the next morning wedged behind the back shelf, spine-down, pages open to the passage about Khorvan's divine heroism. It had been placed as far from the reading light as physically possible.

Pella replaced it on the front shelf. The next morning, it was in the back corner again. She nailed it to the front shelf. The nail bent overnight. She chained it. The chain corroded. She gave up.

"The building has opinions," she tells patrons. "I just work here."

### What the Patrons Think

Most patrons find it charming. Some think Pella is doing it herself for attention (she is not — she has tried everything to stop it). A few scholars from the Free Cities have investigated. One Arcanist detected a faint magical signature — so faint that his instruments couldn't classify it. He left confused.

A Runeweaver examined the building's stones and found something interesting: the warehouse's original foundation stones were salvaged from a much older structure — a Crystalborn waystation that once stood on the same site. The waystation was built from crystalvine-infused granite, which retains a residual capacity to channel Ley Line energy. The library is sitting on a foundation that functions, weakly and intermittently, as a truth-detector.

The crystalvine infusion is so degraded that it can't do much — it can't speak, project images, or perform any active magic. All it can do is exert a faint gravitational pull on objects based on their relationship to factual accuracy. True things are drawn toward it (toward the light, toward the front). False things are repelled (toward the dark, toward the back).

The effect is gentle enough that it only works on lightweight objects — books, scrolls, loose papers. A stone tablet wouldn't move. A person wouldn't feel it. But books, left overnight, slowly migrate.

### The Back Corner

The most interesting thing about the library is not the books in front (which are merely accurate) but the books in the back corner. Because the sorting is not based on *intent* — it's based on *truth content*. A well-meaning but inaccurate history ends up in the middle. A cynically written but factually correct merchant's ledger ends up in front. And a book that contains the most concentrated falsehood in the collection ends up in the farthest, darkest corner.

The *Scripture of the Radiant Dawn* is always in that corner. Always spine-down. Always open to the same passage.

Pella once, out of curiosity, placed a temple-approved children's history of the Ascension on the shelves — a cheerful, illustrated primer titled *The Gods Who Made Us*, donated by the local priest. The next morning, it had migrated to the back corner, spine-down, wedged beside the *Scripture of the Radiant Dawn*. Pella assumed it was because the book was simplified for children — "not wrong exactly, just... imprecise." She put it back on the front shelf. It returned to the corner overnight. She put it back again. Same result.

She keeps it in the back corner now. She tells the children to use the grown-up reference texts instead — the ones about natural history and geography that the library keeps in the front, in the best light.

Pella does not conduct experiments anymore. She says the building's preferences "make her nervous." But she has quietly moved her personal journal — in which she writes honest daily observations about weather, townspeople, and the price of fish — to the front shelf, where it sits in the best light, as if the building approves of her.

### Player Connection

- The library can be visited during Free Cities exploration
- The sorting mechanic is initially a curiosity — the player notices books in unusual positions
- Investigating the foundation reveals the Crystalborn connection
- The player can use the library as a truth-testing device — placing documents or artifacts on the shelves overnight and checking their position in the morning
- This mechanic allows the player to verify lore documents: genuine pre-Ascension texts migrate to the front; Veiled Hand forgeries drift to the back
- Pella, if befriended, shares her experiment with "The gods created the world" — a quiet, devastating lore moment delivered without drama

## 8. The Dwarf Who Tattooed the Stars

**Type:** Runeweaver legend / astronomical mystery
**Region:** Undermountain Holds, the hall of Anvil's Rest
**Era:** Year ~1,350 After Ascension

### The Story

Bram Deepscribe was a Runeweaver's Runeweaver. Where other practitioners carved runes into metal and stone, Bram carved them into himself. Over a lifetime of practice, he covered his body in functional runes — hundreds of them, layered over decades, each one tested and perfected before being permanently inscribed.

His arms bore fire-resistance runes (he could reach into a forge barehanded). His legs bore endurance runes (he could march for three days without rest). His chest bore a ward against poison (he was famously immune to bad ale). His back bore a map of the Undermountain Holds' tunnel system, etched in runes that updated as new tunnels were carved.

Bram was practical. Every rune served a function. He did not collect runes for vanity — each one earned its place on his skin through proven usefulness. His colleagues respected him for his discipline. His apprentices feared him for his standards. His friends (few, but loyal) loved him for his dry humor and absolute honesty.

In Year 1,348, Bram began researching a rune that was not practical.

He had found it in the oldest archives of the Runeweavers' Guild — a symbol recorded in a script that predated the Ascension by centuries. The archive's notation described it as *'The Eye That Looks Backward'* and classified it as "decorative, non-functional, historical curiosity."

Bram disagreed. The symbol's structure was too complex for decoration. Its proportions were too precise for ornament. It followed the mathematical grammar of functional runes, but in a dialect so old that modern Runeweavers couldn't parse it.

He spent two years deciphering it. He consulted Primordialist elders (who recognized elements of the symbol as Primordial script). He studied astronomical charts (the symbol contained star-positioning references). He cross-referenced with the oldest dwarven records (which contained similar symbols carved into mine shafts dug before the Ascension).

On the evening of the winter solstice, Year 1,350, Bram tattooed the rune onto his left palm.

### What Happened

Bram walked out of his workshop and into the night. He raised his left hand toward the sky, palm up, the way a man might hold a lantern.

The stars moved.

Not for long. Not for everyone. For perhaps three heartbeats, for Bram alone, the stars rearranged themselves. Points of light slid across the black sky like beads on an abacus, settling into a new configuration — a pattern that ancient star charts, buried in pre-Ascension ruins, call **the Seven Empty Chairs**.

Seven stars, arranged in a ring. But the chairs were not empty. Seven shapes sat in them — vast, luminous, and *wrong*. They were too small for their seats. They flickered between two forms: one radiant and divine, the other hunched, mortal, afraid. As if the stars were showing him a portrait that kept slipping between the painted surface and the canvas beneath.

And in the center of the ring, where no star shone — a gap. An absence shaped like a door.

The pattern held for three heartbeats. Then the stars returned to their normal positions. Bram lowered his hand. His palm was bleeding where the rune had burned into his skin, scorching through the tattoo ink and into the flesh beneath.

He bandaged his hand. He went back inside. He sat at his desk and wrote a single line in his journal: *"The chairs are not empty. But the things sitting in them do not fit."*

### What Happened After

Bram did not attempt the rune again. The burn on his palm healed into a scar shaped like the rune itself — a permanent mark that tingles when he stands near Ley Line convergence points.

He showed the scar to three people: his apprentice, the Primordialist elder who had helped with the translation, and a senior member of the Ashen Circle. All three agreed: the rune is a *key* — a way to read the Original Seven's hidden signatures in the world's structure. What Bram saw — shapes that didn't fit their seats, flickering between divine masks and mortal faces — shook all three of them in different ways. The Primordialist said nothing for a long time, then: "You saw them as they are. Not as they want to be seen."

Bram is still alive (dwarves live long). He serves as a consultant to the Ashen Circle on pre-Ascension runic systems. He does not perform the rune publicly. He does not tattoo it on others. He says: "I asked the sky a question. The answer was bigger than the question. I'm still working out what it means."

His apprentice, a young dwarf named Kella Brightchisel, has been studying the rune independently. She believes she can modify it to hold the vision longer — long enough to see the mortal faces clearly, to identify who exactly sits on each throne.

She has not yet attempted this. She is, sensibly, afraid of what she might see.

### Player Connection

- Bram can be encountered in the Undermountain Holds as a Runeweaver master
- He teaches advanced runic techniques
- At high reputation, he shows the player the star-rune and explains what he saw
- The player can attempt the rune — revealing the Seven Chairs and the ill-fitting shapes that occupy them, a visual lore confirmation of the Ascended's stolen divinity
- Kella's modification (holding the vision long enough to see the mortal faces clearly) becomes available during the endgame — identifying each Ascended by their true mortal appearance

## 9. The Confession Tree

**Type:** Sacred-site mystery / information cache
**Region:** Holy Empire of Solanthis, the Temple of Verath in the city of Ashenmount
**Era:** Year ~700 After Ascension to present

### The Story

In the courtyard of the Temple of Verath in Ashenmount — a modest temple in a modest city, not the grand cathedral of the capital — stands a tree. It is old. Its trunk is thick with age, its bark silver-white, its roots cracking the courtyard flagstones. It is an oak, or something like an oak, though its leaves shimmer between green and gold in a way that ordinary oaks do not.

The people of Ashenmount come to the tree to confess.

This is not an official practice. The Temple of Verath offers formal confession through its priests, as all temples do. But the people prefer the tree. They press their palms against the bark and whisper. They tell the tree things they would never tell a priest — not because they distrust the priests (they don't, mostly) but because a tree doesn't judge. A tree doesn't lecture. A tree doesn't assign penance.

The tradition is old enough that no one questions it. Parents bring children. The children bring their children. "Tell it to the tree," mothers say when a child is guilty and miserable. "The tree hears, and the tree forgives, and what is told to the tree goes no further."

The priests tolerate it. Most view it as a harmless folk custom — a psychological release that supplements their formal services. The tree is beautiful. It provides shade. It attracts visitors. Why interfere?

### What the Tree Is

The tree is a Truthtree — one of the Ley Line plants described in [10-flora-and-fauna.md](10-flora-and-fauna.md). It grows on a minor Ley Line convergence beneath the temple courtyard, its roots tapping directly into the magical current. Its silver-white bark and green-gold leaves are diagnostic. It is real, and it is doing what Truthtrees do: storing memories.

Every confession whispered to the tree for the last eight hundred years has been absorbed into its growth rings. The tree remembers everything. Every petty theft admitted in a whisper. Every affair confessed through tears. Every lie acknowledged in a hoarse voice. The tree holds eight centuries of a city's secrets — the complete moral inventory of Ashenmount and its people.

Most of it is mundane. Unremarkable sins from unremarkable people. But not all of it.

### What the Tree Contains

The Temple of Verath in Ashenmount is a minor posting — not a career destination but a waystation for priests on their way up or their way down. Over eight centuries, hundreds of priests have served there. Most were ordinary. A few were exceptional. And a few — a very few — were something else entirely.

Three Veiled Hand operatives have been posted to Ashenmount over the centuries. The Veiled Hand embeds agents in temples to monitor for dangerous knowledge. These agents live as priests, perform priestly duties, and maintain their cover for years or decades.

And sometimes, late at night, when the weight of their double life becomes too heavy, they go to the tree.

The tree contains, among its eight hundred years of ordinary confessions:

- A Veiled Hand operative from Year 900 who confessed to assassinating an Ashen Circle scholar and who whispered, through sobs: "She was right. About the thrones. About all of it. And I killed her anyway."

- A Veiled Hand operative from Year 1,100 who confessed to burning a cache of Thornhaven documents and who added, barely audibly: "Thornhaven. The name haunts him. Even the God of Justice can't escape it."

- A senior Veiled Hand operative from Year 1,350 — the most significant confession — who leaned against the bark for an hour and spoke at length about what she knew. She described the cover-up in detail. She named the Ascended by their mortal names. She described the Purge of Memories. She explained the Masks. She whispered everything she had carried for thirty years of service, and then she straightened up, wiped her eyes, and returned to her duties.

She did not know the tree was listening. None of them did.

### The Problem

A Primordialist who could read the tree's growth rings would find the most complete account of the Ascended's true history ever stored in a single location — not from a scholar's research or a prophet's vision but from the guilty consciences of the people who maintain the lie.

No Primordialist has ever examined this particular tree. Truthtrees are known to exist, and the Veiled Hand has standing orders to destroy any that are older than a thousand years. This tree is only eight hundred years old — below the threshold. More importantly, its diagnostic features are muted: growing in an urban stone courtyard with degraded Ley Line access, the tree's silver bark has weathered to a dull grey, and its green-gold leaf shimmer is faint enough to pass for ordinary sunlight on wet leaves. Without active Primordialist attunement, it looks like a pretty old oak — nothing more.

The Veiled Hand's own agents confessed to the very thing the Veiled Hand would destroy if they found it. The irony is perfect: the tree that the cover-up's agents used for emotional relief has become the most comprehensive record of the cover-up in existence.

### Player Connection

- The tree is encountered during Holy Empire exploration
- A Primordialist companion or high Nature attunement reveals the tree's true nature
- Reading the growth rings requires a multi-day ritual (a quest step)
- The confessions are delivered as memory-fragments — the player experiences them from the perspective of the confessor
- The senior operative's confession is one of the most detailed lore reveals in the game — delivered not as a dramatic revelation but as a tired woman leaning against a tree and quietly telling the truth because she couldn't carry it anymore

## 10. The Girl Who Drew the Gods Wrong

**Type:** Child prodigy mystery / divine revelation
**Region:** Holy Empire of Solanthis, the city of Goldenvale
**Era:** Present day

### The Story

Lira is nine years old. She lives in Goldenvale with her mother (a seamstress) and her father (a chandler). She attends temple school. She eats too many honeycakes. She is, in every observable way, an ordinary child.

She draws.

All children draw. Most children draw stick figures, lopsided houses, and suns with faces. Lira draws with a precision that makes artists twice her age uncomfortable. Her lines are clean. Her proportions are accurate. Her shading suggests a understanding of light and shadow that she has never been taught.

The temple school's art instructor noticed her talent early and encouraged it. Lira drew flowers, animals, buildings. She drew her parents. She drew her classmates. Everything she drew was technically remarkable but emotionally normal — the work of a gifted child, not a disturbing one.

Then she drew the gods.

### The Drawings

The temple school assigns a standard project each year: "Draw your favorite god." Children produce earnest, clumsy depictions of Khorvan with his sword, Aelith with her flowers, Selvara with her vines. Template images are provided — the official iconography, reproduced in every temple and scripture across the continent.

Lira did not use the template.

She drew Khorvan first. Not the bronze-armored war god of the official imagery. She drew a thin man in a soldier's uniform two sizes too large, cowering behind a shield, his face a mask of terror. His hands were shaking. His eyes were wide. Behind him, barely visible, were the silhouettes of soldiers marching *toward* a battle he was clearly running *from*.

The art instructor saw the drawing and assumed Lira was being rebellious. She assigned Lira a new god. Lira drew Orinthas — not as the serene, galaxy-eyed sage of the scriptures, but as a young man, barely older than a boy, wearing a crown too heavy for his head. He was standing in front of a burning building, grinning with the vacant confidence of someone who has no idea what he's done. Books were burning in the background. His grin was the most disturbing part — not cruel, not mad, just *empty*. The grin of someone who has never once doubted himself.

The instructor confiscated both drawings. She showed them to the temple priest. The priest showed them to his superior. The superior contacted the regional office.

By the time Lira drew her third picture — Aelith, depicted not as the luminous mother-figure of the scriptures but as a beautiful woman walking away from three small children reaching for her, her face perfectly composed and perfectly cold — the Inquisition had been notified.

### What People Think

The temple maintains that Lira has been "influenced by heretical material." Her parents' home has been searched. No forbidden texts were found. Her parents are devout — they attend services, pay tithes, and own no books beyond the standard household scripture.

The art instructor believes Lira is "channeling unconscious psychological material" — that the drawings reflect fears or family dynamics projected onto divine figures. This is the most reasonable explanation and the one most people accept.

The Inquisition's local representative — a young, ambitious officer named Brother Cassien — believes Lira is "a vessel of the Void Serpent" and has recommended "spiritual cleansing and supervised re-education." This recommendation has not yet been approved, partly because Lira's parents have connections in the Chandlers' Guild and partly because Brother Cassien is widely considered overzealous.

Lira herself, when asked why she draws the gods "wrong," says: "I don't draw them wrong. I draw them how they look."

When asked where she sees them, she says: "When I close my eyes. They're always there. Not the big shiny versions. The real ones. The real ones look sad."

### What Lira Is

Lira is not possessed. She is not a prophet. She is not channeling the Void Serpent or receiving transmissions from the Unmaskers.

Lira carries divine resonance.

The same rare trait that allowed the seven Ascended to find the Threshold and claim the thrones exists, in weaker form, in a handful of mortals in every generation. Most never know they have it. It manifests as unusual sensitivity — a feeling of unease in temples, a tendency to hear faint music near Ley Line convergences, an ability to sense emotions in enchanted objects.

In Lira's case, the resonance manifests as *sight*. When she closes her eyes and thinks of the gods, she doesn't see the official projections — the Masks, the divine personas, the carefully crafted images. She sees *through* them, to the mortal faces beneath. Not clearly — she's a child with an untrained gift — but clearly enough to draw what she sees.

She draws Khorvan cowering because she sees the coward behind the war god's mask. She draws Orinthas as a grinning fool because she sees the fool behind the sage's mask. She draws Aelith walking away from children because she sees the woman who abandoned her own.

Lira does not understand what she's drawing. She doesn't know the gods' mortal histories. She doesn't know about the Ascension, the Masks, or the cover-up. She is nine years old. She draws what she sees because that is what artists do.

The Veiled Hand has not yet become involved. The local Inquisition report has not been flagged as significant — it is filed under "minor heretical art, likely psychological projection, recommend monitoring." If a Veiled Hand analyst ever reads the report and looks at the drawings, they will recognize — instantly, viscerally — what they're looking at.

They will recognize the mortal faces of the gods, drawn by a child who has never seen them, because the truth is written in the world's bones and some people are born able to read it.

### Player Connection

- Lira can be encountered during Holy Empire exploration in Goldenvale
- She initially appears as a minor NPC — a child drawing in a temple courtyard
- Speaking with her reveals her unusual art; the player can view her drawings
- A player with divine resonance (which the player character carries) recognizes what the drawings show — an immediate, wordless "I see it too" moment
- Protecting Lira from the Inquisition becomes a side quest if Brother Cassien escalates
- Lira's drawings, when cross-referenced with the Ascended's true histories (learned through other questlines), provide visual confirmation — she has drawn faces the player can now match to names
- At endgame, Lira's gift suggests that divine resonance is becoming more common — that the world itself is producing people who can see through the Masks, as if preparing for the time when the truth will emerge whether the gods want it to or not
