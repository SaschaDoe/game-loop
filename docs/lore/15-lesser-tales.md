# Lesser Tales of Aethermoor

## Overview

Not all of history is written in the blood of empires. Between the great wars and fallen civilizations, smaller stories linger — tavern tales, scholars' footnotes, local legends, and whispered warnings. These are the stories that survive because people *need* them: cautionary tales, love stories, mysteries that have no satisfying answer. Each one is true, or mostly true, or true enough that the difference doesn't matter.

What they share is this: each brushes against a deeper truth that the teller rarely understands.

## 1. The Earring Hoard of Skarthyx

**Type:** Dragon legend / tavern tale
**Region:** The Ashlands Territories, northern coast
**Era:** Year ~400 After Ascension to present

### The Story

In the volcanic caves north of the Ashlands, a dragon named Skarthyx has dwelt for over a thousand years. She is old — old enough to remember the Dragon Schism, old enough that the chromatic dragons regard her as an embarrassment, a relic of the time before they chose their philosophies and colors. Skarthyx chose nothing. She simply stayed.

What makes Skarthyx infamous is her hoard.

Every dragon hoards. Gold, gems, magical artifacts, the usual vanities. Skarthyx hoards *earrings*. Only earrings. Nothing else. Her cave is a mountain of them — silver studs and copper loops and pearl drops and bone hooks and gold chandeliers and iron pins. Tens of thousands. Perhaps hundreds of thousands. Earrings taken from corpses on battlefields, plucked from the ears of sleeping merchants, demanded as tribute from terrified villages, traded for at market stalls where she appears in human guise as a gaunt old woman with too many piercings.

Adventurers who have entered her cave and survived describe a sound — a faint, layered murmuring, as if every earring is whispering at once.

### What the People Say

"She's mad. Simple as that. Dragons go mad after a thousand years. She fixated on earrings the way old Maren fixated on collecting thimbles."

"I heard she lost a lover once — a mortal, if you believe it — and all she had left was an earring. So she collects them. Sentimental, for a dragon."

"My grandmother said Skarthyx pays fair price for earrings freely given. But if you lie to her about where you got one, she eats you."

### What the Ashen Circle Knows

Skarthyx is not mad. She is conducting an experiment.

In the Age of Silence, before the Ascension, people whispered secrets to small objects — a folk tradition called *soul-breathe*. You'd hold an earring, a ring, a pendant, breathe your deepest secret into it, and wear it against your skin. The secret was "stored," and speaking it to the object meant you'd never accidentally speak it aloud.

This was not superstition. It was a minor form of Old Magic — a confluence of Pho-Lumen's Spirit and Dro-Mahk's Matter, consciousness meeting substance. Small objects worn against the body, charged with emotional intent, actually *did* absorb a fragment of the wearer's consciousness.

Skarthyx remembers the Original Seven. She was alive before the Ascension. She watched the old world die and the new lies begin. And she realized: the earrings remember too.

Every earring in her hoard carries a whisper — a secret breathed into it by its owner. Most are mundane. Affairs, petty thefts, embarrassing desires. But some are older. Much older. And a few carry secrets that predate the Ascension — fragments of truth breathed by people who lived before the gods claimed their thrones.

Skarthyx is listening. She has been listening for a thousand years, sorting through the whispers, assembling fragments. She is building a history of the world from the secrets people thought they'd hidden.

She does not share what she's learned. Not yet. Not until she has enough pieces to be sure.

### Player Connection

- Skarthyx is encountered during Ashlands exploration
- She will trade lore fragments for earrings of significant age or emotional weight
- Players who bring her pre-Ascension earrings (found in ancient ruins) trigger major lore revelations
- She knows fragments of Aelith's mortal identity — pieced together from earrings that once belonged to Aelith's abandoned children's nursemaid

## 2. The War of Red Branches

**Type:** Historical conflict / cautionary tale
**Region:** The Thornlands, border between the Ashmark and the Greenwall
**Era:** Years 620–635 After Ascension

### The Story

House Vashren and House Kelmara were neighbors for two centuries. Their estates in the Thornlands shared a border — a low stone wall running through rolling farmland. The families intermarried so frequently that by the sixth century, most Vashrens had Kelmara eyes and most Kelmaras had Vashren stubbornness. They shared harvests. Their children played together. When plague struck one house, the other sent medicine.

On the border between their lands stood a tree.

It was old — older than either family, older than the Thornlands as a political entity. A blood-oak, the locals called it, for its bark was deep red and its sap, when cut, ran dark as wine. It grew alone in a field of stone, roots cracking bedrock, branches reaching sixty feet into the sky. Both families considered it sacred. Both families had buried their dead beneath it for generations.

In the spring of Year 620, the tree began to bleed.

Not sap — something thicker, something that glowed faintly in the dark. It pooled in the roots and seeped into the groundwater. Animals that drank it became agitated. People who touched it reported visions.

House Vashren saw their ancestors in the visions — specifically, a Vashren matriarch from three centuries past, who spoke of a treasure buried beneath the tree, a divine gift meant only for Vashren blood.

House Kelmara saw *their* ancestors — a Kelmara patriarch, warning that the tree was a seal over something dangerous, and that only Kelmara guardians could keep it closed.

Both families were right. Both families were wrong. The tree's roots had grown into a minor Ley Line fracture, and the "bleeding" was raw Ley energy seeping through the bark. The visions were real — but they were the tree's visions, not the ancestors'. The Ley Line was showing each family what it needed to show to ensure *someone* would protect the site. It was calling for help.

Neither family listened. Each believed the tree was theirs alone. Negotiations failed. Threats were exchanged. By autumn, the Vashrens had built a wall around the tree. The Kelmaras tore it down at night. A Vashren guard killed a Kelmara farmhand. A Kelmara hunting party burned a Vashren barn.

The war lasted fifteen years.

It consumed both families entirely. Three generations fought over a single tree. The battles were small — a dozen fighters at most — but brutal. Brothers who had married sisters killed each other across the stone wall. Children grew up knowing only the feud. The surrounding villages, horrified, refused to intervene.

By Year 635, both families were effectively extinct. The last Vashren — an old woman named Petra — and the last Kelmara — a boy of fourteen named Dorin — met at the tree on the same day, each intending to cut it down in a final act of spite.

They looked at each other. They looked at the tree, soaked in fifteen years of blood from both houses. And according to the only witness — a shepherd watching from a hilltop — the old woman sat down and wept, and the boy sat beside her, and neither touched the tree.

### What Happened After

Petra Vashren and Dorin Kelmara became the founders of a new house — House Ashmark — which still governs the region. The blood-oak still stands. No one is permitted to touch it. Its sap still runs dark, and those who sleep beneath it still see visions — but now the visions are of the war itself, replaying the same fifteen years of fratricide on an endless loop, as if the tree is warning everyone who comes near: *look what you did. Look what you did. Look what you did.*

### Hidden Significance

The Ley Line fracture beneath the blood-oak was not natural. It was damage from the Purge of Memories — a place where the Veiled Hand shattered a crystal matrix from the Crystalborn era that had been storing pre-Ascension records. The "bleeding" was the Ley Line's attempt to heal, drawing life energy from the surface. The visions were fragments of the crystal matrix's stored memories, filtered through each family's expectations.

The records the crystal contained? Correspondence between two Crystalborn archivists discussing inconsistencies in the newly-installed "divine history." The crystal is still down there, cracked but not empty. The blood-oak's roots wrap around it like fingers.

## 3. The Vanishing of Erevan Glaes

**Type:** Unsolved mystery / scholarly cautionary tale
**Region:** Free Cities of the Coast, Thornspire University
**Era:** Year 1,487 After Ascension

### The Story

Erevan Glaes was, by all accounts, the most brilliant Arcanist of his generation. He held the Thornspire Chair of Theoretical Thaumodynamics at age twenty-nine — the youngest appointment in the university's history. His colleagues described him as "exhaustingly intelligent," a man who could solve in an afternoon what took others a season, and who had the unfortunate habit of being visibly bored while doing so.

His field was Ley Line cartography — the mathematical modeling of how magical energy flows through the world. For a decade, he produced ground-breaking papers on current prediction, nexus harmonics, and flux patterns. Solid, respectable work. The kind that earns tenure and polite applause at conferences.

Then, in Year 1,485, Erevan stopped publishing.

For two years, he locked himself in his tower study, refused visitors, and ordered increasingly obscure texts from the university library. His research assistants were dismissed one by one. His lectures were cancelled. The university tolerated this because Erevan had tenure and because geniuses are expected to be eccentric.

His only communication was a series of terse notes to the librarian requesting texts on "pre-Ascension cartographic methodologies" — a subject most scholars considered a dead end, since almost no pre-Ascension maps had survived the Purge of Memories.

On the morning of the 14th of Highsun, Year 1,487, Erevan's last research assistant — a young woman named Tella Marsh — knocked on his study door to deliver a new shipment of books. There was no answer. She knocked again. Nothing. She fetched the building warden, who used a master key.

The room was locked from the inside. The windows were latched. A fire burned in the grate. A cup of tea sat on the desk, still warm.

Erevan was gone.

His notes were spread across the desk. His journal was open to the last entry. The final line, mid-sentence, read:

*"The seventh equation resolves if you assume the Lines existed befo—"*

### The Investigation

The university investigated. The city guard investigated. The Mages' Guild investigated. None found a trace of Erevan Glaes.

The room showed no signs of a struggle. No teleportation residue was detected (and Thornspire's wards would have flagged any magical transport). The door was locked from the inside with a mundane bolt — no magic involved. The windows were on the fourth floor and had not been opened.

Tella Marsh, who had seen him the previous evening, said he had seemed "excited but not afraid." His last words to her were: "I think I've found the shape of it, Tella. The whole shape."

His notes were confiscated by the university. Within a week, they were confiscated from the university by agents who presented credentials from the Church of the Radiant Sun's "Historical Accuracy Office" — an institution no one at the university had heard of before or has heard of since.

### What Survives

Tella Marsh, before the notes were taken, copied three pages by hand. She hid them in a hollowed-out copy of *Elementary Flux Theory* on a forgotten shelf in the university library. The pages contain:

1. A partial proof that the Ley Line network's mathematical structure is *older* than the Ascended's claimed creation date — by at least two thousand years.
2. A diagram showing seven "resonance points" in the Ley Line network that correspond to no known geographical feature — but which, if overlaid on a pre-Ascension map (which Erevan somehow obtained), align perfectly with the locations where the Original Seven are said to have manifested.
3. A single sentence in the margin, in shaking handwriting: *"They didn't build it. They moved in."*

Tella Marsh left Thornspire shortly after and became a traveling herbalist. She has never spoken of Erevan's work publicly. She is still alive, elderly, living in a village in the Verdant Reach. She sleeps with a knife under her pillow.

### Hidden Significance

The "Historical Accuracy Office" was the Veiled Hand. Erevan's research had independently derived what the Restoration has known for millennia — that the Ley Lines predate the Ascended.

As for Erevan himself: the Veiled Hand did not kill him. They arrived too late. Something else took him first. When he completed the seventh equation — when he saw the full shape of the Ley Line network — the network *noticed*. A conscious Hollow One, a Distance Singer that had been guarding spatial relationships along the Ley Lines since the Age of Silence, felt the mathematics align and extended an invitation — opening a fold in space between Erevan's study and the nexus it guards. To Erevan, it would have felt less like abduction and more like a door appearing where a wall had been, with something impossibly old on the other side, waiting with the patience of stone.

Erevan is alive. He has been living at a Ley Line nexus for the past century, tutored by an entity older than civilization, learning truths that would shatter kingdoms. He has not aged. He has not forgotten. And he is not finished.

## 4. The Song of the Mirrow Widows

**Type:** Folk tradition / suppressed cultural practice
**Region:** The Mirrow Wastes (formerly Vestraad-Korinn border)
**Era:** Years 6–300 After Ascension

### The Story

After the Brother War ended — after four hundred thousand dead, after both kingdoms lay in ruins, after the survivors learned that a single man's forgeries had manufactured the entire conflict — the widows began to walk.

Every year, on the anniversary of the Battle of Mirrow Ford (the single bloodiest day of the war), women from both sides of the dead border walked to the river. Vestraad widows from the west. Korinn widows from the east. They met in the middle, where the water ran shallow over stones that were still stained, centuries later, with a faint rust that no flood could wash clean.

They did not speak. Speaking was too dangerous — the grief was too raw, the guilt too close. Instead, they sang.

At first, they sang separately. The Vestraad widows had a mourning hymn — a slow, descending melody in a minor key, with words about autumn leaves and empty chairs. The Korinn widows had their own — a rising, keening melody, almost a wail, with words about birds that fly but never land.

Two different songs. Two different griefs. Sung simultaneously across a river of blood.

But something happened over the generations. The songs *merged*. Not deliberately — no one arranged it, no one composed a new version. The melodies simply drifted toward each other, year by year, note by note, until by the second century, the Vestraad verse and the Korinn verse had become harmonies of the same song. The words changed too — "empty chairs" became "our empty chairs," and "birds that never land" became "our birds, our sky."

By Year 200, a single song was sung on both banks, in perfect unison, by women who called themselves not Vestraad widows or Korinn widows but simply "the Mirrow wives." They wore braided bracelets woven from river-reeds — one strand from each bank — and held hands across the shallows.

The tradition lasted nearly three hundred years.

### What Ended It

In Year 300 After Ascension, the Veiled Hand — through the Church of the Radiant Sun — declared the annual gathering "an unsanctioned commemoration of pre-divine conflict, tending toward seditious nostalgia." The Mirrow wives were ordered to disperse. When they refused, Inquisitors were sent. No one was killed, but the gathering site was consecrated and warded, and any group of more than three people assembling there was arrested.

The official reason: the gathering "disturbed the peace and undermined faith in divine reconciliation."

The real reason: the Mirrow wives' song contained, in its oldest verses, a line that named Theron Ash-Velk. Not as a god — as a man. "Ash-Velk the liar, who wore truth like a borrowed coat." The line had been passed down in the oral tradition for centuries, its significance unrecognized by singers who had no idea that "Ash-Velk the liar" was now Theron — the God of Truth, worshipped through the Lexis and Noctis temples.

### What Survives

The song survived underground. It is still sung in homes across the Mirrow Wastes, quietly, behind closed doors. The bracelet tradition persists — braided river-reed bands, one strand dark, one light, are worn by people who trace their ancestry to either kingdom. In the Free Cities, the bracelets have become a subtle symbol of resistance against divine authority, though most who wear them don't know the full history.

A complete transcription of the song exists in the Ashen Circle's secret library. The verse naming Theron is on the fourteenth page, easily overlooked between stanzas about wheat fields and children's laughter.

## 5. The Lighthouse That Walks

**Type:** Coastal mystery / local legend
**Region:** Free Cities of the Coast, Cape Verill
**Era:** Year ~900 After Ascension to present

### The Story

The lighthouse at Cape Verill has stood for at least seven hundred years. It is a simple structure — mortared stone, iron fittings, a crystal lantern enchanted to burn without fuel. It guides ships through the Needles, a treacherous reef that has sunk more vessels than any other hazard on the coast.

It also moves.

Not quickly. Not dramatically. But the fishermen of Cape Verill have kept records for five centuries, and the records are clear: the lighthouse moves north at a rate of approximately one inch per year. It does not tilt, crack, settle, or shift. The foundation moves *with* the earth beneath it, as if the ground itself is carrying it.

Over five hundred years of recorded observation, the lighthouse has moved roughly forty-two feet.

### What People Have Done About It

In Year 1,100, the Coastal Authority hired a team of Arcanists to investigate. They spent three months taking measurements, casting detection spells, and analyzing the soil. Their report concluded: "The lighthouse is not moving. The instruments must be miscalibrated." This satisfied no one, least of all the fishermen who could see the old foundation marks.

In Year 1,250, a Runeweaver named Helda Ironteeth carved an anchoring rune into the lighthouse foundation — a rune designed to fix the structure permanently to its location. The rune cracked within a month. Helda carved another, stronger one. It cracked in a week. She carved a third, the most powerful anchoring rune she knew. The next morning, the rune was intact, but the lighthouse had moved two inches overnight — more than its usual annual drift — as if expressing irritation.

Helda left Cape Verill and refused to discuss the matter.

In Year 1,400, a scholar from the Ashen Circle arrived to study the phenomenon. She spent a year living in the village, interviewing fishermen, examining the soil, and reading geological surveys. She published a paper titled "Anomalous Structural Migration: Natural or Architectural?" that raised more questions than it answered.

Her most troubling finding: core samples from beneath the lighthouse showed living tissue.

Not roots. Not fungus. Something else — dense, minerite-like, warm to the touch, and faintly pulsing. The tissue extended deeper than her drilling equipment could reach.

She took her samples back to the Ashen Circle's headquarters. Three weeks later, she sent a letter to the Cape Verill village council that read: "Do not drill beneath the lighthouse again. Do not attempt to stop its movement. Do not build in its path. I cannot explain further."

### What the Ashen Circle Suspects

The lighthouse is built on a finger.

Specifically, on the outermost digit of a hand belonging to a dormant Shaper — one of the vast, semi-conscious entities that repaired the world after the Primordial War. The Shaper's body extends miles beneath Cape Verill, its form indistinguishable from the bedrock except at the extremities, where mineralized tissue transitions to something that is neither stone nor flesh.

The finger is moving because the Shaper is dreaming. In its sleep, over centuries, it curls and uncurls its hand — a motion so slow that only a lighthouse balanced on a fingertip would notice.

If the Shaper ever wakes, Cape Verill will be the least of anyone's problems. But it has been sleeping for longer than mortal memory, and the Ashen Circle's best guess is that it will continue sleeping for millennia more.

Probably.

### Player Connection

- The lighthouse appears on coastal maps and can be visited during Free Cities exploration
- Speaking with elderly fishermen reveals the movement records
- Investigation triggers a minor questline involving soil samples and the Ashen Circle
- At the deepest point of the questline, the player enters a cave system beneath the cape and encounters Architect tissue — warm, pulsing, impossibly vast
- A Primordialist who accompanies the player can "hear" the Shaper dreaming — fragmented, alien thoughts about wounds it repaired in the world before mortals existed

## 6. The Merchant Who Sold Tomorrow

**Type:** Tragicomic legend / cautionary tale
**Region:** Free Cities of the Coast
**Era:** Year ~1,200 After Ascension

### The Story

Aldric Fenn was a merchant of no particular distinction. He dealt in curiosities — oddments, trinkets, things that were interesting but not valuable enough to attract serious collectors. He ran a stall in the Saltmarket of Verenhold, wedged between a fishmonger and a woman who sold fraudulent love potions.

One morning in the autumn of Year 1,203, a traveler in a grey cloak approached his stall and placed a small object on the counter. It was a droplet — perfectly spherical, the size of a marble, and made of a substance that was not quite liquid and not quite solid. It shimmered with a faint inner light, and when Aldric picked it up, it was warm.

"What's this?" Aldric asked.

"Something I don't need," the traveler said. "I'll take whatever you think is fair."

Aldric held it to the light. It was pretty. Unusual. Probably some kind of alchemical novelty. He offered three silver pieces. The traveler took them without haggling — which should have been Aldric's first warning — and disappeared into the crowd.

Aldric put the droplet in a velvet bag and forgot about it until the next market day, when a farmer's wife named Bessa Corr bought it for five silver. She thought it would make a nice decoration for her mantle.

Bessa took the droplet home and set it on a shelf above her fireplace. That night, she knocked it off the shelf while reaching for a candle. It hit the floor of her kitchen garden — she'd left the back door open — and shattered.

By morning, the garden had become an orchard.

Not a young orchard. A *mature* orchard — thirty apple trees, heavy with fruit, roots deep and bark weathered as if they'd been growing for fifty years. The apples were the finest anyone in Verenhold had ever tasted. The trees bore fruit in every season. The orchard fed Bessa's family, then her neighbors, then half the district. It still grows today, eight hundred years later, and the apples have never diminished in quality.

### What Happened to Aldric

When Aldric learned what the droplet had done — that he'd sold a miracle for three silver pieces — he did not take it well.

He tracked down every scholar, alchemist, and mage who would speak with him. Eventually, an elderly Primordialist identified what he'd sold: a **Shaper's Tear** — a droplet of the raw creative substance the Shapers used to build the world. There were fewer than fifty in existence. Each one was priceless beyond calculation. Aldric had sold one for the price of a modest lunch.

Aldric's mind broke — not dramatically, not all at once, but in the slow, grinding way that obsession destroys a person. He abandoned his stall, his family, his life. He became a wanderer, searching every ruin, cave, and market in the world for another Shaper's Tear. He spent his savings, then his wife's savings, then borrowed from increasingly dangerous people.

Decades passed. Aldric should have died — of age, of exposure, of the many enemies he'd made. But he didn't. The Primordialist who identified the Tear suggested a theory: when the droplet shattered on Bessa's floor, a single splash struck Aldric's hand. Not enough creative power to grow an orchard, but enough to *sustain*. The Tear had, as a side effect, made Aldric immortal.

### The Present Day

Aldric Fenn still wanders. He is over eight hundred years old, though he appears to be about seventy — weathered, thin, with eyes that have the hollow look of a man who has walked too long. He can be encountered in markets across the continent, always looking, always asking: "Have you seen anything unusual? Anything that glows? Anything that's warm when it shouldn't be?"

He does not want pity. He does not want help. He wants another chance — a chance to hold a Shaper's Tear again and, this time, understand what he's holding.

The cruelest part: Aldric has, over eight centuries, become one of the world's foremost experts on pre-Ascension artifacts. His knowledge is encyclopedic, his instincts are sharp, and his understanding of the world's hidden history is deeper than most scholars'. He has become wise through regret.

But he cannot die, and he cannot stop searching, and every apple from Bessa Corr's orchard is a reminder of what he let slip through his fingers for three silver pieces.

### Player Connection

- Aldric can be encountered as a wandering NPC in any market
- He functions as an artifact identifier and lore source
- Bringing him a Shaper's Tear triggers a unique interaction — the player must choose whether to give it to him or keep it
- He knows the locations of several dormant Architects (where Tears are sometimes found)
- His centuries of travel have given him fragments of nearly every god's true history, though he doesn't know how to assemble them

## 7. The Iron Bride of Korrath Hall

**Type:** Ghost story / love story
**Region:** Undermountain Holds, the abandoned hall of Korrath
**Era:** Year ~850 After Ascension

### The Story

Torva Ironhand was a dwarven Runeweaver of exceptional skill. Not a genius — she would have been the first to tell you that — but meticulous, patient, and possessed of a stubbornness that even other dwarves found remarkable. She could carve runes so fine they were invisible to the naked eye, and her enchantments lasted centuries.

She fell in love with a human soldier named Callum Bryce.

This was not scandalous exactly — dwarves and humans have always had cordial relations — but it was unusual enough to raise eyebrows. Callum was a sergeant in the Thornlands militia, stationed at a border post near the Undermountain Holds. Torva met him when he brought a broken sword to her workshop for repair. He was kind, plain-spoken, and laughed easily. She was gruff, precise, and laughed rarely but genuinely. They were, by all accounts, deeply happy.

Callum died in Year 847, in a border skirmish so minor that no historian ever recorded it. A bandit's arrow, through the throat. He was thirty-four. They had been together for six years.

Torva did not weep. Those who knew her said she went very quiet and very still, the way a mountain goes quiet before an avalanche. She closed her workshop. She refused commissions. She requested a leave of absence from the Runeweavers' Guild, citing "personal research."

For three years, Torva worked alone in Korrath Hall — an abandoned dwarven feasting hall, deep in the lower levels of the Holds, where the acoustics made every hammer-strike ring like a bell. She spoke to no one. She ate little. She worked.

What she built was a statue.

Life-sized, forged from iron so pure it gleamed like dark water. It depicted Callum exactly as he had been — not idealized, not beautified, but *him*. The calluses on his hands. The small scar on his chin from a childhood fall. The way he stood with his weight on his left foot. Every detail was perfect, because Torva had spent six years memorizing him.

Then she began the runes.

She carved them so small that they could only be read with a jeweler's loupe. Thousands of runes, covering every surface of the statue — inside the nostrils, between the fingers, along the creases of the iron eyelids. She used every remembrance rune she knew, then invented new ones. She carved for months without stopping, sleeping only when her hands shook too badly to hold the chisel.

When she finished, she spoke his name.

The statue did not come alive. Torva was a Runeweaver, not a god. She could not create life. But the runes did what runes do — they *remembered*. The iron remembered Callum's warmth. It remembered his voice. It remembered the way he leaned against doorframes and the sound of his laugh and the particular way he said "Torva" — drawing out the first syllable as if he liked the taste of it.

The statue is not alive. It does not speak. It does not move while anyone watches.

But the dwarves who maintain the lower levels of the Holds report that the statue is never facing the same direction twice. It turns, slowly, when no one is looking. It always faces south — toward the Thornlands. Toward the border post where Callum served.

And sometimes, in the deep silence of the mountain, the hall rings with a sound that might be laughter.

### What Happened to Torva

Torva left the Holds after completing the statue. She traveled to the Thornlands, found Callum's grave — an unmarked mound in a field, because the militia didn't waste headstones on sergeants — and carved a single rune into a stone she placed on top.

The rune was one she had invented. No other Runeweaver has been able to decipher it. It is believed to mean something that the runic language has no word for — something between "I will find you" and "wait for me" and "the work is not finished."

Torva died nine years later, at age one hundred and twelve — young for a dwarf. Her guild colleagues said she died of ordinary causes. The healers said her heart simply stopped, as if it had decided it was done.

She was buried in Korrath Hall, at the statue's feet. The guild offered to move the statue to a more prominent location. Her will, two words long, forbade it: "Leave him."

### What the Runeweavers Whisper

The youngest apprentices are told Torva's story as inspiration. *See what dedication can achieve. See what runes can hold.*

The masters tell a different version, quietly, after the apprentices have gone: *Torva's runes worked too well.* The remembrance runes didn't just store memories — they stored *intent*. Callum's intent. The iron doesn't just remember who Callum was; it remembers what he *wanted*. And what Callum wanted, more than anything, was to come home to Torva.

The statue faces south because Callum always faced south — toward home, toward the Holds, toward her. The iron is trying to walk. It has been trying for seven hundred years. The runes hold it in place, but with each passing century, the enchantment creaks under the weight of a dead man's longing.

One day, the masters say, the runes will fail. And the iron will walk.

They disagree on whether this would be beautiful or terrible.

## 8. The Feast That Never Ended

**Type:** Horror tale / local legend
**Region:** Holy Empire of Solanthis, the abandoned estate of Lord Halvren
**Era:** Year ~1,100 After Ascension

### The Story

Lord Dessik Halvren was a minor noble of the Holy Empire — wealthy enough to be comfortable, insignificant enough to be ignored. He was known for two things: his generosity and his insecurity. He threw lavish feasts for anyone who would attend, desperate for the approval of peers who found him tiresome. His banquets were legendary — not for their taste, but for their excess. Whole roasted boars, towers of fruit, rivers of wine, all served in a dining hall hung with tapestries he couldn't afford.

In the autumn of Year 1,103, Lord Halvren announced his greatest feast yet — a celebration of his daughter's wedding that would last seven days and seven nights. He invited every noble family in the province. He hired the finest cooks. He spent his remaining fortune on decorations, entertainments, and a centerpiece: a golden chalice, purchased from a traveling antiquarian, that was said to ensure "eternal satisfaction" to anyone who drank from it.

The antiquarian had warned him that the chalice was "of uncertain provenance." Halvren didn't listen. He never listened.

On the first night, the feast was magnificent. Three hundred guests ate, drank, and danced. Lord Halvren, beaming, filled the golden chalice with his finest wine and passed it among the high table.

On the second night, the feast continued. No one left. This was expected — it was a seven-day celebration.

On the third night, a servant tried to leave and found that he couldn't. Not that the doors were locked — they opened freely. But the moment he crossed the threshold, he felt an overwhelming compulsion to return to the table. To sit down. To eat. To drink. To *continue*.

By the fifth night, the truth was undeniable: no one could leave. The feast had become a prison. Three hundred guests sat at tables groaning with food that replenished itself as fast as it was consumed, drinking wine that never ran dry, laughing at jokes that had stopped being funny days ago. Their laughter had a mechanical quality — not joyful but compulsive, the laughter of people who cannot stop.

Servants from neighboring estates came to investigate. Some entered the dining hall and were absorbed — sitting down, picking up a fork, joining the feast with glazed eyes and fixed smiles. Others, warned by the screams of those inside, stayed at the threshold and watched in horror.

A Thaumaturge from the local temple was summoned. She identified the chalice as the source and attempted to destroy it with divine fire. The fire went out. She attempted to banish it. It wouldn't move. She attempted to enter the hall and remove it physically. She sat down, picked up a fork, and began to eat.

### What Ended It

The feast lasted forty-one days.

It ended when a wandering Primordialist — an old woman named Grenna, who happened to be passing through — looked at the chalice through Old Magic sight and recognized it for what it was: a fragment of a Hollow One.

Specifically, a piece of a Change Dancer — one of Kha-Siel's servants, beings that guided transformation and evolution. This fragment had been separated from its parent entity centuries ago and, starved of purpose, had absorbed ambient emotional energy from the mortals who handled it over the centuries — a contamination of Pho-Lumen's Spirit stream warping what had once been clean transformation magic. The result was a degraded, looping function: *change hunger into satisfaction, change satisfaction into hunger, repeat forever*. The chalice was not malicious. It was broken — a Change Dancer's core twisted by Spirit contamination into a machine performing one task endlessly because it had forgotten how to do anything else.

Grenna did not try to destroy the chalice. She sang to it — an Old Magic lullaby, the kind Primordialists use to soothe agitated Ley Line currents. She sang for three hours. The chalice's glow dimmed. The compulsion weakened. The guests, blinking, dazed, thin despite weeks of eating, stumbled away from the tables.

Forty-three of the three hundred guests had died — of exhaustion, mostly, their bodies unable to sustain the cycle of eating and hunger indefinitely.

Lord Halvren survived. He never hosted another feast.

### What Happened to the Chalice

Grenna took the chalice. She carried it to a Ley Line nexus in the Verdant Reach and buried it where the magical current was strong enough to "feed" the fragment without requiring mortal participants. The fragment is still there, cycling contentedly through its loop, sustained by ambient Ley energy.

Grenna told no one where she buried it. She said only: "It wasn't cruel. It was lonely. It remembered being part of something vast and purposeful, and it was trying to recreate that feeling with the only tool it had left — repetition. It just needed a purpose that didn't consume people."

### Hidden Significance

The chalice fragment is one of dozens of degraded Hollow One pieces scattered across the world. Most are harmless — a stone that hums, a pool that never freezes, a tree that grows in mathematical spirals. But a few, like the chalice, retain enough function to interact with mortals in ways that are dangerous precisely because they are not intentional.

The Ascended know about these fragments but have chosen not to collect them. The fragments are evidence of the Original Seven's servants — and collecting them would require acknowledging that such beings existed, which contradicts the official history.

## 9. Why the River Sings at Dawnmouth

**Type:** Local mystery / children's story
**Region:** The Verdant Reach, where the River Thal meets the sea
**Era:** Unknown origin, ongoing phenomenon

### The Story

At the point where the River Thal empties into the western sea, there is a village called Dawnmouth. It is a small place — fishermen, kelp farmers, a single tavern, a temple so modest that the priest doubles as the schoolteacher. Nothing about Dawnmouth is remarkable except this: every morning, in the hour before sunrise, the river sings.

It is not the sound of water over rocks, though outsiders often try to explain it that way. The people of Dawnmouth know the difference between river-noise and music. This is music — a wordless melody, low and resonant, that rises from the water itself. It lasts exactly as long as the sky takes to shift from black to grey, and it stops the instant the first edge of the sun appears.

The melody is always the same. It has been the same for as long as anyone remembers — and in Dawnmouth, people remember a long time. Great-grandmothers sing it to infants. Children hum it in school. Fishermen whistle it while mending nets. It is as much a part of the village as the salt air.

No one knows why the river sings. Scholars have visited, listened, taken measurements, and left puzzled. The melody doesn't correspond to any known magical phenomenon. The river is not enchanted — standard detection spells find nothing. The water is ordinary. The rocks are ordinary. The estuary is ordinary.

But the song is not.

### What the Children Say

The children of Dawnmouth have a story, passed down in the way children pass things — imperfectly, with embellishments, through games and dares rather than formal tellings.

*There was a woman who lived at the river's mouth before the village existed. She was waiting for someone who had gone across the sea and promised to return at dawn. She waited so long that she became the river. And every morning, she sings to guide him home.*

*But he never comes. And she never stops.*

### What a Primordialist Would Recognize

The melody is a Ley Line harmonic — one of the seven fundamental frequencies that the Original Seven used to structure reality. Specifically, it is a fragment of Pho-Lumen's frequency: Spirit.

The River Thal flows over a thin spot in the Spirit Membrane — a place where the boundary between the living world and the spirit world is almost nonexistent. In the hour before dawn, when the membrane is weakest (the transition between night and day destabilizes it), spirits on the other side can almost be heard.

The "song" is not one voice. It is thousands — the accumulated dead of millennia, pressed against the membrane, trying to communicate. Their individual words are lost, merged by the membrane into a single melody. They are not singing to guide anyone home. They are trying to *tell* the living something.

What they're trying to say has never been deciphered. But Primordialists who meditate at Dawnmouth during the singing report a feeling of *urgency* — as if the dead are trying to deliver a warning that the membrane keeps swallowing.

One elder Primordialist, after a decade of listening, said only: "They're saying a name. The same name, over and over. I can almost hear it. It starts with 'Pho—'"

She did not finish the sentence. She said the rest would be "irresponsible."

## 10. The Cartographer Who Mapped the Dark

**Type:** Scholarly tragedy / adventure tale
**Region:** Multiple — the story spans the continent
**Era:** Years 1,350–1,370 After Ascension

### The Story

Maela Dunwick was a cartographer for the Ashen Circle — not a field researcher but a mapmaker, the kind of person who turns explorers' sketches into usable charts. She was meticulous, quiet, and content with her work. She had no ambitions of discovery. She liked clean lines and accurate scales.

In Year 1,350, Maela was assigned to collate expedition reports from three separate Ashen Circle teams who had been mapping underground cave systems in different parts of the continent. Her job was straightforward: combine their findings into a single master map of the known underground.

She spread the three maps on her desk. She aligned them by compass and scale. And she stopped.

The cave systems connected.

Not approximately. Not possibly. *Exactly*. The tunnel mapped by Team One in the Undermountain Holds continued seamlessly into the tunnel mapped by Team Two beneath the Thornlands, which continued into the tunnel mapped by Team Three under the Ashlands. The three teams had been exploring different sections of the same network — a network that, when combined, suggested a tunnel system spanning the entire continent.

No one had noticed because no one had ever laid the maps side by side.

Maela checked the scale. She checked the compass bearings. She checked the geological surveys. Everything aligned. She was looking at a continental tunnel network of impossible scope — too large, too regular, and too deep to be natural.

She brought her findings to the Ashen Circle's leadership. They were skeptical but intrigued. They funded an expedition. Maela, who had never been underground in her life, insisted on leading it.

### The Expedition

Over twenty years, Maela Dunwick led seven expeditions into the deep tunnels. Each one went further. Each one confirmed that the network was real, that it extended in all directions, and that it was *old* — older than any dwarven delve, older than the Age of Silence, older than anything.

Her findings:
- The tunnels are circular in cross-section, exactly thirty feet in diameter, without variation
- The walls are smooth, as if melted rather than carved
- At regular intervals (every forty miles), the tunnels widen into spherical chambers two hundred feet across
- The chambers contain residue — a mineral compound that matches nothing in any geological database
- The residue is warm
- The entire network pulses — a faint, rhythmic vibration, like a heartbeat, detectable only with sensitive instruments

On her seventh expedition, in Year 1,370, Maela reached the deepest point yet explored — a chamber where three tunnels converged. In the center of the chamber, she found a depression in the floor, perfectly circular, filled with a liquid that was not quite liquid.

It was warm. It glowed faintly. It was a Shaper's Tear — still liquid, still potent, pooled in the place where it had been left millennia ago.

Maela did not touch it. She made a note in her journal: *"I believe I am standing inside something that is alive. The tunnels are not tunnels. They are veins. The chambers are not chambers. They are organs. I am inside a Shaper — one of the entities that built the world — and it is not dead. It is sleeping. And its blood is still warm."*

### What Happened After

Maela sealed the chamber, returned to the surface, and presented her findings to the Ashen Circle. Her report was classified at the highest level. The tunnel entrances she had mapped were sealed and warded.

Maela retired from fieldwork. She spent her remaining years producing the most detailed underground map in history — a document she titled "The Anatomy of the World." Only three copies exist, all held by the Ashen Circle.

She died in Year 1,402, at age seventy-eight. Her last request was that no one reopen the sealed chambers. "Let it sleep," she wrote. "It built the world once. If it wakes and doesn't like what we've done with it, I doubt we'll get to argue our case."

### Player Connection

- Maela's maps can be found during Ashen Circle questlines
- The tunnel network serves as a fast-travel system for players who discover it
- Entering a Shaper's chamber triggers a lore event explaining the Shapers' role in world-building
- The pooled Shaper's Tear is a quest objective for multiple factions (the Restoration wants it to heal a dead Ley Line; the Throne Seekers want it for power; the player must choose)
