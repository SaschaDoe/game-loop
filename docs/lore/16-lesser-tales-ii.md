# Lesser Tales of Aethermoor — Volume II

## Overview

More stories from the margins of history. These are the tales that don't appear in any official chronicle — because the chroniclers didn't know them, or because knowing them was dangerous. They survive in tavern songs, in grandmothers' warnings, in the private journals of people who saw something they shouldn't have and were wise enough to write it down before they forgot.

Or before they were made to forget.

## 1. The Twin Thrones of Gallowmere

**Type:** Political tragedy / cautionary tale
**Region:** Holy Empire of Solanthis, the province of Gallowmere
**Era:** Years 1,180–1,196 After Ascension

### The Story

King Aldren Voss of Gallowmere had twin sons: Edric and Oswin. He loved them equally, which was his first mistake. He promised the crown to whichever proved himself worthier, which was his second. He died without clarifying what "worthier" meant, which was his third and last.

Edric was the elder by eleven minutes. He was steady, cautious, and well-liked by the common folk. He listened before he spoke. He married a farmer's daughter because he loved her, which scandalized the court and delighted the kingdom.

Oswin was the younger. He was brilliant, restless, and adored by the nobility. He could quote law and history from memory, negotiate a trade deal in three languages, and hold a room's attention simply by entering it. He married a duchess from a neighboring province, securing a powerful alliance.

Both had legitimate claims. The kingdom split — not violently at first, but in the quiet, corrosive way that divided loyalties eat at a nation's bones. Northern lords backed Edric. Southern lords backed Oswin. The middle provinces tried to stay neutral and were pressured by both sides.

For six years, the brothers negotiated. Proposals were made: shared rule, alternating years, a division of the kingdom. Every proposal failed because each brother's advisors convinced him that compromise was weakness, and weakness invited conquest from neighboring powers.

In Year 1,186, Oswin's wife was found dead — poisoned, according to the court physician. Oswin blamed Edric. Edric denied it. Evidence was produced: a vial of poison in the rooms of Edric's steward, a confession extracted under duress, a pattern of payments traced to Edric's treasury.

Edric produced counter-evidence: the vial was planted, the confession forced, the payments forged. He accused Oswin of murdering his own wife to manufacture a casus belli.

The war that followed lasted ten years. It consumed Gallowmere entirely. Edric's popular army fought Oswin's professional army to mutual exhaustion. Harvests rotted in fields because the farmers were dead. Children grew up knowing their uncle's name as a curse word.

In Year 1,196, both brothers died on the same day — not in battle, but by their own hands. Each received a letter, delivered by a rider neither court recognized, containing proof that the other brother had not killed Oswin's wife.

The letters contained the truth: Oswin's wife had been killed by a woman named Vera Silke, an agent of the Veiled Hand's Division of Narrative. The accompanying documents — in Vera Silke's handwriting, sealed with the Veiled Hand's cipher — detailed the operation: the assassination, the planted evidence, the forged counter-evidence, the careful escalation designed to keep both brothers fighting for as long as possible.

The purpose: beneath Gallowmere's capital lay the ruins of a Crystalborn archive — one of the last intact crystal matrices from before the Purge of Memories. The Veiled Hand needed a decade of war to keep everyone too busy to notice the excavation happening under the city. By the time the brothers received their letters, the archive had been emptied and its contents destroyed.

Edric and Oswin each read the letter. Each understood what had been done — not just to them, but through them. Each wrote a single-line response and sent it to the other.

Edric's note read: *"Brother, I am sorry."*
Oswin's note read: *"Brother, forgive me."*

The notes crossed in transit. Neither was read until servants found the bodies.

### What Survives

Gallowmere never recovered. The province was absorbed into the Holy Empire proper, its identity dissolved into administrative districts. The ruins of the capital still stand — two throne rooms, one north, one south, both empty.

The letters from Vera Silke were hidden by a servant named Pol, who couldn't read but knew they mattered. He buried them in a clay jar beneath the floor of his cottage. The cottage still stands, occupied by Pol's descendants, who have been told by each generation: "Never dig under the hearthstone. Never. Not for anything."

They don't know why. But they don't dig.

### Hidden Significance

The Crystalborn archive beneath Gallowmere contained crystal recordings of pre-Ascension diplomatic correspondence — including letters between the Crystalborn and the Iron Republics discussing "the empty thrones beneath the earth" centuries before the Ascension. The letters proved that mortal civilizations knew the thrones existed before the Ascended claimed them — demolishing the official mythology that the Ascended created the thrones themselves.

The Veiled Hand destroyed everything. Or so they believe. One crystal — small, cracked, overlooked — rolled into a drainage gutter during the excavation and was washed into the city's ancient sewers. It is still there, wedged between stones, glowing faintly in the dark, holding a single recorded conversation between two scholars about "the seven seats that wait for no one."

## 2. The Blood Singer Who Heard Too Deep

**Type:** Disappearance / mystery
**Region:** The Ashlands Territories
**Era:** Year ~1,300 After Ascension

### The Story

Ysha Redthorn was not the most powerful Blood Singer of her generation. She was not the most talented, the most learned, or the most feared. What she was, uniquely, was the most *sensitive*.

Blood Singers hear the pulse. Every practitioner of blood magic learns to feel the rhythm of life — their own heartbeat first, then others', then the pulse of living things around them. A trained Blood Singer can sense the heartbeats of every creature within a hundred paces. The best can extend that range to a mile.

Ysha could hear heartbeats across a continent.

She described it as a roar — a constant, overwhelming ocean of rhythm. Every heart that beat in the world beat in her ears. She had to learn, as a child, to filter the noise the way a person living near a waterfall learns to sleep through the thunder. Her teachers thought it was a curse. Ysha thought it was beautiful.

She catalogued what she heard. Human hearts beat differently from elven hearts. Dwarven hearts were slower and deeper. Dragon hearts shook the air like drums. She could identify species, age, health, and emotional state from a heartbeat alone. She became the foremost diagnostician in the Ashlands — able to identify illness in a patient three towns away by listening to the irregularity in their pulse.

In the autumn of Year 1,302, Ysha heard something new.

It was not a heartbeat. Not exactly. It was slower — one beat every several minutes — and deeper than anything she had ever detected. It didn't come from a direction. It came from *below*. Deep below. So deep that she could feel it more in her bones than in her ears.

She told her mentor, an old Blood Singer named Kael, that she'd found "the heartbeat of the earth."

Kael told her to stop listening.

Ysha did not stop.

Over six months, she tracked the rhythm. She walked the Ashlands with her eyes half-closed, following the pulse the way a dowser follows water. It grew stronger when she moved toward certain geological formations — ancient rock, deep stone, places where the earth was old and undisturbed.

Her journal from this period survives. The entries grow progressively stranger:

*Month 1: "The heartbeat is regular. One beat every four minutes and twelve seconds. It never varies. Whatever this is, it has been beating for a very long time."*

*Month 3: "I've mapped the strongest resonance points. They form a pattern — too regular to be geological, too vast to be biological. Unless the biology is on a scale I haven't considered."*

*Month 5: "The heartbeat changed today. For the first time in six months, it skipped. Just once. Then resumed. I felt it in my teeth."*

*Month 6 (final entry): "I know what it is. I have to go down. There's an entrance in the deep caves east of the Ashmark. I can feel the pulse strongest there — it's like standing on the chest of something impossibly large. I'm not afraid. I think it's been waiting for someone to listen."*

Ysha Redthorn entered the deep caves on the 8th of Deepwinter, Year 1,303. She left her journal, her pack, and her coat at the entrance, as if she expected to be gone only a short while.

She has not been seen since.

### What Kael Knew

Old Kael, when pressed by Ysha's family, said only: "The girl heard the Shaper's heart. No one was supposed to hear that. The Shapers are sleeping. They're *supposed* to be sleeping. And when something that big rolls over in its sleep, the last thing you want is to be the noise that woke it up."

He refused to say more. When asked if Ysha was dead, he said: "I don't think dead is the right word. I think she's listening. I think the Shaper is listening back. And I think that conversation is going to last longer than either of us will."

### Hidden Significance

The heartbeat Ysha heard matches the pulsing rhythm described decades later by Maela Dunwick in her cartographic expeditions (see [15-lesser-tales.md](15-lesser-tales.md), Story 10). The cave entrance Ysha used connects to a different branch of the same continental tunnel network Maela mapped — the vein system of a dormant Shaper. Maela's expedition teams, entering from different locations, never reached the Ashlands section where Ysha descended.

Ysha is alive. Like Erevan Glaes, she was drawn to something ancient that recognized her sensitivity. Unlike Erevan, she went willingly. The Shaper did not take her — it *welcomed* her, the way a sleeping body might welcome the touch of a warm hand. She exists now in a symbiotic state with the Shaper, her blood magic sustaining a link between her human consciousness and the Shaper's vast, slow dreaming mind.

She has learned things no living mortal knows. The Shaper remembers the world's construction. It remembers the Original Seven as living presences, not abstract principles. It remembers the Void Serpent's attack and the desperate repair work that followed.

It also remembers the moment seven strangers sat on seven thrones and the world's song went wrong.

### Player Connection

- Ysha's journal can be found at the cave entrance during Ashlands exploration
- Following her path leads deep into the Shaper's vein network
- At the deepest point, the player encounters Ysha — alive, ageless, half-merged with the stone, her eyes glowing with the Shaper's slow pulse
- She can teach advanced Blood Magic techniques unavailable anywhere else
- She offers fragments of the Shaper's memory — the oldest, most direct account of the Ascension available in the game

## 3. The Ship That Sails the Fog

**Type:** Ghost story / sailor's legend
**Region:** The western coast, the Hollow Sea
**Era:** Pre-Ascension (the ship's departure); Year ~600 After Ascension (first documented sightings) to present

### The Story

Every sailor on the western coast knows the *Pale Herald*. Not by sight — no one who has seen it will describe it willingly. By reputation. By the way old captains go quiet when the fog rolls in thick and wrong, when it smells not of salt but of deep water and something older.

The *Pale Herald* is a ship. It sails without a crew. Or rather, it sails with a crew that isn't alive — not ghosts exactly, not undead exactly, but something in between. Figures at the railings, translucent and blue-lit, moving with the mechanical precision of people performing duties they've performed for a thousand years. They do not speak. They do not acknowledge the living. They sail.

The ship appears only in dense fog — the kind that rolls in without warning and muffles sound as if the air itself has thickened. Fishermen who have glimpsed it through the murk describe a vessel of alien design: too long, too narrow, built from materials that aren't wood or metal but something that looks like solidified water. Its sails — if they are sails — ripple without wind.

The *Pale Herald* always moves in the same direction: toward the center of the Hollow Sea.

### What the Sailors Say

"Don't follow it. Don't signal it. Don't even look at it longer than a breath. It's not for us. It's going somewhere we can't follow, and if you try, the fog takes you."

Three ships have tried to follow the *Pale Herald*. Two were found weeks later, adrift, their crews alive but unable to remember anything after entering the fog. The third was never found.

"My grandfather said the *Herald* appears more often now than when he was young. Used to be once a decade. Now it's every year, sometimes twice. Something's changed. Something's calling it more urgently."

### What the Ashen Circle Has Pieced Together

The *Pale Herald* is a vessel of the Sunken Dominion — the submarine civilization that controlled the deep oceans before it was swallowed by dimensional anomalies in the Hollow Sea (see [09-civilizations-risen-and-fallen.md](09-civilizations-risen-and-fallen.md)).

The ship was dispatched from Pelagathis, the Dominion's capital, in the final days before the city sank into a dimensional rift. Its mission: to carry a warning to the surface nations about what was happening in the deep ocean. The crew — five officers and forty sailors — volunteered, knowing the journey was likely one-way.

They never arrived. The dimensional instability that swallowed Pelagathis caught the *Pale Herald* mid-voyage. The ship was trapped between dimensions — not fully in the mortal world, not fully in the rift. The crew, caught in the transition, became neither alive nor dead. The ship, caught between realms, became visible only when the boundary between dimensions thinned — in fog, in storms, in the uncertain hours between midnight and dawn.

The *Pale Herald* has been trying to complete its mission for over a thousand years. It sails toward the center of the Hollow Sea because that is where Pelagathis fell — the ship is trying to reach its home port, not realizing that the port no longer exists in any reachable dimension. The crew continues their duties because they are bound by oath to deliver their warning. They cannot stop. They cannot deviate. They cannot die until the message is delivered.

The warning they carry — inscribed on a tablet of volcanic glass sealed in the captain's quarters — describes what the Sunken Dominion saw happening to the Ley Lines beneath the ocean in the years before the Ascension: the Lines were *rearranging themselves*, as if something were preparing to take control of them. The Dominion's scholars recognized this as a prelude to a seizure of divine power, and the warning was their attempt to alert the surface world.

The warning arrived a thousand years too late.

### Why It's Getting Worse

The *Pale Herald* appears more frequently because the dimensional boundary in the Hollow Sea is thinning. This is connected to the Void Serpent's cage weakening — the same instability that created the rift is worsening as the cage deteriorates. If the boundary thins enough, the *Pale Herald* will finally cross fully into the mortal world — along with everything else trapped in the rift, including the ruins of Pelagathis and whatever has been living in them for the past millennium.

The Leviathan Thalassion, which maintains the ocean currents near the Hollow Sea, has altered its patrol pattern in the last century — avoiding the area where the rift is weakest. Leviathans do not frighten easily.

### Player Connection

- The *Pale Herald* can be encountered during coastal or ocean travel
- A questline involving the ship requires the player to enter the fog and board the vessel
- The captain's quarters contain the volcanic glass tablet — a major lore artifact
- Reading the tablet reveals the Sunken Dominion's warning about the Ley Line rearrangement before the Ascension
- The player can choose to deliver the warning to the crew's intended recipient (a descendant of the original surface nation's government), finally releasing them from their oath

## 4. The Portrait That Speaks at Midnight

**Type:** Ghost story / art legend
**Region:** Free Cities of the Coast, the Verenhold Gallery of Masters
**Era:** Year ~900 After Ascension (the painting); ongoing (the phenomenon)

### The Story

The Verenhold Gallery of Masters is the finest art collection in the Free Cities — three hundred paintings, sculptures, and tapestries spanning a thousand years of artistic achievement. It is housed in a converted merchant's palace, lit by enchanted lanterns, and guarded by wards that have protected the collection through two fires, one siege, and three attempted thefts.

In the east wing, in a room that the gallery staff call the Quiet Room, hangs a single portrait.

The painting depicts a woman. She is elderly — perhaps seventy — with sharp features, dark eyes, and hands folded in her lap. She wears a scholar's robe of the kind that hasn't been fashionable for centuries. Behind her, through a window, a city is visible — towers of crystal catching the light, a sky full of prismatic reflections. A city that no one has been able to identify.

The portrait is unsigned. The medium is oil on canvas — ordinary materials. The technique is extraordinary: the brushwork is so fine that the woman's face seems to breathe, to shift with the light, to age and un-age as the viewer moves. Art scholars have studied it for centuries and cannot determine the method.

The painting is kept in its own room because of the sound.

At midnight — exactly midnight, by the gallery's mechanical clock — the portrait speaks.

Not loudly. Not clearly. A murmur, as if the woman in the painting is continuing a conversation that was interrupted centuries ago. The words are in a language that no one at the gallery recognizes — a language that scholars from three universities have failed to identify.

The speech lasts exactly three minutes. Then silence until the next midnight.

### What People Have Tried

In Year 1,050, a linguist named Heron Dusk spent six months recording the portrait's nightly speech. He transcribed the sounds phonetically, filling nine notebooks with careful notation. He sent copies to every language department in the Free Cities.

No one could identify the language.

In Year 1,200, a Thaumaturge attempted to commune with the portrait using divine magic. The magic failed — not blocked, not reflected, simply... irrelevant. The painting existed in a space where divine magic had no purchase. The Thaumaturge described it as "trying to light a candle in a room that predates fire."

In Year 1,380, a Primordialist elder visited the gallery at midnight. She listened to the portrait speak. She went very pale. She said: "That is Celestial. The high register — the form used for scholarly discourse among the Crystalborn elite. It has been dead for two thousand years."

She was able to translate fragments — not all, because the dialect was older than any she had studied, but enough. The woman in the portrait was a scholar. She was describing her research. She was discussing Ley Line harmonics — specifically, the mathematical relationship between the seven major Ley Lines and the structure of what she called "the seats of principle."

The thrones.

She was discussing the thrones — centuries before the Ascension. She was discussing them as known objects of study, not as divine mysteries. She was saying, in the tone of a scholar reviewing established literature: *"The seats predate the civilization that studies them. They predate civilization itself. They are structural features of the world's magical architecture — load-bearing elements, if you will. They were not built. They emerged from the Lines' self-organization, the way crystals emerge from solution. They wait. They have always waited. The question is not who will sit. The question is what sitting will cost."*

The Primordialist elder did not publish her translation. She memorized it, burned her notes, and left Verenhold the same night. She told no one — except a single Ashen Circle contact, who recorded it in a cipher only three living people can read.

### What the Painting Really Is

The portrait was painted by a Crystalborn artist using crystal-infused pigments — a technique lost with the Crystalborn civilization. The pigments contain micro-crystalline structures that store and replay information, the same technology used in the Archives of Light. The portrait is, in essence, a recording device disguised as a painting.

The woman in the portrait was real — a Crystalborn scholar named Thessa Kel-Varin, who lived approximately two hundred years before the Ascension. The crystal city visible through the window is the Crystal Citadel. The three minutes of nightly speech are a fragment of a lecture she gave on Ley Line architecture — recorded by the pigments and replayed in an infinite loop.

Thessa Kel-Varin's lecture predates the Ascension by two centuries. Her discussion of the thrones as natural, structural features of the world contradicts every aspect of the official mythology, which claims the Ascended created the thrones as seats of divine authority.

The portrait survived the Purge of Memories because no one recognized it as a recording. It looks like a painting. It sounds like a ghost. The Veiled Hand has investigated the Quiet Room twice and concluded both times that the phenomenon was "a minor haunting of no significance."

They were wrong.

### Player Connection

- The gallery can be visited in Verenhold during Free Cities exploration
- The player can listen to the portrait at midnight
- With high enough Primordialist attunement or Celestial language skill, the player can translate the speech
- The translation is a major lore document proving the thrones predate the Ascended
- The Ashen Circle offers a follow-up quest to find other crystal-pigment paintings that may contain additional recordings

## 5. The Well of Wet Answers

**Type:** Local mystery / village legend
**Region:** The Thornlands, the village of Kettle
**Era:** Year ~800 After Ascension to present

### The Story

The village of Kettle has a well. This is not remarkable — every village has a well. What is remarkable is that Kettle's well answers questions.

The tradition is simple and has been followed for as long as anyone can remember: write your question on a piece of parchment, fold it twice, and drop it into the well. Wait until the next morning. Lower a bucket. The bucket will contain your parchment, now soaking wet, with an answer written on the back in a cramped, hurried script.

The answers are always truthful. They are also always brief, sometimes cryptic, and occasionally rude.

Sample exchanges from the village archives (yes, they keep records):

*Q: "Will the harvest be good this year?"*
*A: "Plant earlier. The frost comes late."*
(The frost came three weeks late that year. Those who planted early had their best harvest in memory.)

*Q: "Is my husband faithful?"*
*A: "Define faithful. He thinks about the baker's wife but has done nothing. Whether thoughts count is a question for a priest, not a well."*

*Q: "What is the meaning of life?"*
*A: "You've wasted a perfectly good piece of parchment."*

*Q: "Who are you?"*
*A: "Someone who made a mistake."*

*Q: "What mistake?"*
*A: "I asked a question I should not have asked, and was answered in a way I could not refuse."*

*Q: "When will you be free?"*
*A: "When someone comes who can hear the answer I am not allowed to write."*

### What the Village Knows

The people of Kettle do not question the well. It helps them. It has helped them for eight hundred years — predicting weather, diagnosing illness (with surprising accuracy), settling disputes, and occasionally warning of danger. When a band of raiders approached the village in Year 1,100, the well's answer to the morning's question ("Should we worry about strangers on the north road?") was: *"Yes. Twelve armed men, arriving at dusk. Put the children in the root cellar and send Yorn to the garrison."* The warning saved the village.

The well is Kettle's most valuable asset and its deepest secret. Outsiders are not told about it. The tradition is kept within village families, passed from parent to child. The village council has a standing rule: no scholar, no priest, and no one from the cities is ever to be shown the well. This rule has been followed for centuries.

When asked why, the village elder says: "Because the well asked us not to."

### What a Primordialist Would Recognize

The well is built on a Spirit Membrane thin-spot — a place where the barrier between the living and the dead is nearly nonexistent. The cramped, hurried handwriting belongs to someone on the other side of the Membrane — a spirit, conscious and articulate, trapped in the boundary layer.

A Primordialist who examined the well's magical signature would find something unusual: the spirit is not a ghost in the conventional sense. Ghosts are echoes — fading impressions of the dead, usually confused, usually fixated on their moment of death. This spirit is *lucid*. It thinks, reasons, adapts, and grows. It has been learning for eight hundred years. It knows things no ghost should know — current events, future weather, the movements of living people it has never met.

This is because the spirit is not merely dead. It is *bound* — held in the Membrane by a soul-binding ritual of the type used during the Purge of Memories (see [08-great-wars.md](08-great-wars.md), Phase 2: The Spirit Silencing). It was a witness — someone who knew the truth about the Ascended — and the Veiled Hand's Division of Silence bound it into the earth rather than risk an exorcism that might scatter the knowledge.

The binding was not meant to last this long. It was a temporary containment — hold the spirit while we decide what to do with it. But the Division of Silence operative who performed the binding was killed in a skirmish a week later, and the report was lost. No one came back. The binding persisted, maintained by the Ley Line energy flowing through the thin-spot.

Eight hundred years. Conscious. Unable to leave. Unable to die. Unable to do anything but answer the questions of villagers and wait.

### Who They Were

The spirit's identity is never directly revealed through the well — it cannot write its own name (a restriction of the binding). But the clues in its answers, accumulated over centuries of village records, paint a picture:

- It was a scholar, from the way it writes and reasons
- It lived during the early Ascension period (it once referenced "the new gods" as if they were recently installed)
- It knew something about the Ascended that the Veiled Hand considered dangerous enough to require binding
- It is patient, kind, and deeply lonely
- It has a particular interest in children's questions, which it answers with more warmth and detail than adults'

The village records contain one exchange that no one in Kettle understands:

*Q (from a child, Year 1,400): "Do the gods love us?"*
*A: "Some of them have learned to. That is not the same thing. But it is not nothing."*

### Player Connection

- The village of Kettle can be discovered during Thornlands exploration
- Earning the village's trust (several side quests) grants access to the well
- The player can ask questions — the well gives gameplay hints, lore fragments, and cryptic warnings
- With Spirit magic or Old Magic attunement, the player can hear the spirit's voice directly — and learn what it knows about the Ascension
- A high-level quest allows the player to break the binding, freeing the spirit — who then delivers the testimony the Veiled Hand tried to bury eight centuries ago

## 6. The Sword That Weeps

**Type:** Artifact legend / soldier's tale
**Region:** The Mirrow Wastes (formerly Vestraad-Korinn border)
**Era:** Year ~50 After Ascension (forging); present (the weeping)

### The Story

After the Brother War, when both the Allied Kingdom of Vestraad and the Allied Kingdom of Korinn lay in ashes, the survivors gathered what was left. There was not much. Two kingdoms' worth of history, reduced to rubble and grief.

A blacksmith named Torren — a man who had lost his sons on opposite sides of the war (one had married into Vestraad, the other into Korinn) — collected the melted remnants of both kingdoms' crowns from the ruins of their throne rooms. The gold was warped and black with smoke. The jewels had cracked in the fires. But the metal remembered what it had been.

Torren forged a sword.

He worked for a year. He was not a master smith — just a village blacksmith who shoed horses and mended plows. But he worked with a grief so focused it burned hotter than his forge. He folded the two crowns' metal together, layer upon layer, until the blade was a ripple of Vestraad gold and Korinn gold, indistinguishable. He set the cracked jewels — rubies from Vestraad, sapphires from Korinn — into the crossguard, alternating. He wrapped the grip in leather from a saddle that had carried soldiers from both sides.

When the blade was finished, he quenched it in the Mirrow River — the same river that had run red with the blood of the Battle of Mirrow Ford.

The sword screamed.

Not the hiss of hot metal meeting water. A scream — human, anguished, multiplied. As if four hundred thousand voices cried out at once through the steam.

Torren dropped the blade and did not touch it again.

### The Weeping

The sword — unnamed, because Torren refused to give it one — was taken by a local lord who thought it would make a fine trophy. He mounted it on his wall. That night, his household woke to the sound of weeping. Quiet, persistent, coming from the sword. Iron tears beaded on the blade and ran down its length, pooling on the floor.

The lord gave it away. The next owner gave it away. And the next. The sword passed through dozens of hands over the centuries, always given away, never sold (those who tried to sell it found the tears increased until the blade was too slippery to hold).

Every owner reports the same experience: the sword weeps. Constantly. Iron tears that never rust, never dry, and carry a faint warmth. And anyone who draws the blade from its scabbard feels, for one searing instant, the collected grief of the Brother War — not as memory but as *experience*. Four hundred thousand deaths, compressed into a single heartbeat of understanding.

Most who draw the blade collapse. Some weep for hours afterward. A few go silent for days.

One woman — a soldier named Caera Vint, in Year 800 — drew the blade and did not collapse. She stood holding it, tears streaming down her face, and said: "I can feel them. All of them. Both sides. They don't want vengeance. They want to understand *why*."

She carried the sword for thirty years, using it to settle disputes along the Mirrow Wastes. She said the sword gave her perfect empathy — she could feel the grief of anyone she faced in judgment. She was considered the fairest arbitrator of her generation.

When she died, the sword was placed in her tomb. It still weeps. Pilgrims visit the tomb to listen. They say the weeping sounds different depending on the listener's own grief — louder for those carrying more, softer for those at peace.

### Hidden Significance

The sword's power is not magical in the conventional sense. It is Old Magic — specifically, a Blood Singer phenomenon. Torren was not a Blood Singer (he had no training), but his grief was so intense and so focused that he performed an unconscious act of blood magic during the forging. His life-energy — saturated with mourning — fused with the metal. The quenching in the Mirrow River, where the blood of the dead had seeped into the water table, completed the process.

The sword is, in essence, a blood-magic artifact created by accident. It stores emotional memory the way memory moss stores cognitive memory. And the emotions it stores — four hundred thousand deaths, the grief of two nations — are fundamentally connected to Theron Ash-Velk's crime. The sword doesn't know this. It simply carries the weight of what Theron caused.

If the sword were ever brought into Theron's presence — into any temple of Lexis, any site sacred to the God of Truth — it would not merely weep. The scholars of the Ashen Circle believe the collected grief of the Brother War, concentrated in one artifact, would resonate with Theron's own guilt powerfully enough to crack his Mask. Briefly. Perhaps for only a moment. But a moment would be enough.

The Veiled Hand knows about the sword. They have chosen not to destroy it — it is too well-known, too beloved by the people of the Mirrow Wastes, and destroying a mourning artifact would raise exactly the kind of questions they want to avoid. Instead, they ensure it stays in Caera Vint's tomb, far from any temple, far from any god.

## 7. The Thief Who Stole a Shadow

**Type:** Underworld legend / heist tale
**Region:** Holy Empire of Solanthis
**Era:** Year ~1,050 After Ascension

### The Story

Mira Quickfingers was the best thief in Solanthis. Not the most famous — that was Darian the Bold, who stole the Lord Mayor's wig during a speech and replaced it with a cat. Mira was better than Darian in every way that mattered: she was quieter, cleverer, and she never got caught.

Her specialty was temples. She robbed them with a precision that drove the Inquisition to distraction. Jeweled censers, golden chalices, silk vestments, rare texts — if a temple had it and it was worth stealing, Mira could take it. She was careful never to steal anything of genuine religious significance (she was practical, not suicidal), and she always left the poor boxes untouched (she was a thief, not a monster).

In the autumn of Year 1,050, Mira received a commission from an anonymous buyer: steal a specific item from the inner sanctum of the Temple of Ferrum — the largest temple to the War God in the Holy Empire.

The item was described as "a shadow pinned to the wall with seven silver nails."

Mira thought this was a metaphor. A tapestry, perhaps, or a painting in dark tones. The buyer offered ten thousand gold — more than Mira had earned in her entire career. She took the job.

Breaking into the Temple of Ferrum took three weeks of preparation. The temple was guarded by paladins, warded by Thaumaturges, and blessed by daily rituals that made the building hostile to anyone carrying ill intent. Mira bypassed the paladins with patience, the wards with a stolen priest's sigil, and the blessing by carrying a genuine prayer scroll against her heart (she didn't believe, but the spell didn't check sincerity — a design flaw she'd exploited before).

The inner sanctum was a circular room of black iron, lit by braziers. On the far wall, between two braziers, she saw the shadow.

It was not a metaphor.

Pinned to the wall by seven silver nails — one through each extremity, one through the chest, one through the forehead, one through the throat — was a shadow. Not a painting of a shadow. Not a projection. A *shadow*, flat and dark against the iron wall, with no object to cast it. It was the silhouette of a man — a thin man, hunched, with his arms raised as if warding off a blow.

The shadow's posture was wrong for a god of war. It was the posture of someone flinching.

Mira pulled the nails. The shadow came off the wall like fabric, folding into her hands. It was cold — colder than anything she'd ever touched. It weighed nothing. It whispered.

She couldn't make out the words. She didn't try.

She delivered the shadow to the anonymous buyer — a woman in a featureless mask who met her in a warehouse at midnight. The buyer examined the shadow, nodded, and paid in full. As Mira turned to leave, the buyer said: "You should know what you've stolen."

"I never want to know what I've stolen," Mira said. "It's policy."

"This is different. This is the mortal shadow of a god. It shows what he was before he sat on his throne. Every god cast off their mortal shadow during the Ascension — the part of themselves they wanted to forget. The temples keep them locked away where no one will see. Because a god's shadow shows a god's true face."

Mira looked at the shadow in the buyer's hands. In the dim warehouse light, the shadow's posture was clearer: a man cringing, cowering, his hands raised not in battle but in surrender. The shadow of a coward.

"Whose shadow is it?" Mira asked.

"Khorvan's," the buyer said. "The God of War. The Valiant. The Unbreaking. His shadow shows what he was — what he still is, underneath. A man who ran while his soldiers died."

The buyer vanished. Literally — one moment there, the next gone, leaving behind a faint scent of old paper and the lingering cold of the shadow.

### What Happened After

Mira retired from thievery. She never said why. Friends who knew her well said she seemed *unsettled* — not frightened, exactly, but disturbed in a way that went deeper than fear.

She opened a bakery in a small coastal town. She lived quietly. She never stole again.

Twenty years later, she told one person — her apprentice baker, a young man named Kell — what had happened. She said: "I've stolen from a hundred temples and never felt guilty. But that shadow... it wasn't an object. It was a *confession*. Pinned to a wall with nails. Whoever put it there wanted it to suffer. And whatever that shadow was, it was afraid. I don't steal from things that are afraid."

### Hidden Significance

The buyer was a senior member of the Unmaskers — specifically, a direct descendant of the Thornhaven legacy. She had been searching for physical evidence of the Ascended's mortal nature for decades. Her knowledge of the shadows came from a single source: a dying Veiled Hand defector — one of the "Ungloved" (see [07-factions-and-orders.md](07-factions-and-orders.md)) — who revealed the shadows' existence and the Ferrum temple's location as a final act of rebellion. Even the defector didn't know the full theology; the buyer was working from fragments and guesses, though her guess proved right. The shadow is exactly what she described: the mortal aspect of Khorvan, cast off during the Ascension and preserved by the temple's high priest (who is a Veiled Hand operative) as a containment measure.

Each Ascended cast off a mortal shadow when they claimed their thrones — the transformation was not complete, and the parts of themselves that the divine power couldn't fully absorb were ejected as these shadow-fragments. The temples that house them don't know their significance (only the highest-ranking Veiled Hand agents do). The shadows are filed under "sacred relics, do not touch, do not question."

The shadow of Khorvan is now in the Unmaskers' possession. It is their single most powerful piece of evidence: a physical manifestation of the God of War's cowardice, visible to anyone who looks at it. They are waiting for the right moment to reveal it.

The Temple of Ferrum reported the theft but classified it as "a minor relic of unknown significance." The Veiled Hand, upon learning what was actually stolen, launched a quiet, frantic search. They have not found it.

Khorvan himself felt the shadow's removal. For one night — the night Mira pulled the nails — the God of War's Mask flickered, and his priests across the empire reported that the Valiant appeared, for a single heartbeat, to be *afraid*.

## 8. The City That Remembers Rain

**Type:** Ruin mystery / desert legend
**Region:** The Ashlands Territories, eastern edge
**Era:** Pre-Ascension ruin; ongoing phenomenon

### The Story

The city has no name. The Ashlands nomads call it *Drem-Ka-Thol* — "the place that mourns water," in their tongue. Travelers call it the Weeping Ruin. Cartographers mark it with a small symbol and the note: "Avoid. Disorienting."

It stands at the eastern edge of the Ashlands, where the desert gives way to cracked red rock. A city of white stone — or what was white stone, before centuries of sand scoured it to bone-grey. Towers, plazas, fountains, aqueducts, gardens (the frames of gardens — the earth is dust). It was beautiful once. It is a skeleton now.

It has not rained in the city or its surrounding area for over a thousand years. The climate shifted after the Ascension — the Ley Line that once sustained the region's weather pattern was redirected by the Ascended as part of their restructuring of the world's magical infrastructure. The city, which had been built around a temperate oasis fed by that Ley Line's influence, dried to nothing within a generation. Its people left. The sand came.

But the city remembers rain.

Every day, at the hour when rain would have fallen — late afternoon, when the desert heat breaks and the air goes still — the city's gutters run with water. Not much. A trickle. Clear, clean water that appears in the channels of the old drainage system, flows through the carved gutters of the streets, fills the bottom inch of the bone-dry fountains, and then... stops. Evaporates. Gone within the hour, as if the memory exhausted itself.

The water is real. It can be drunk (travelers have survived by timing their visits to the afternoon hour). It can be collected (though it evaporates faster outside the city's boundaries, as if reluctant to leave). It is not magical — standard detection spells find nothing. It is not condensation — the desert air is too dry. It is not groundwater — the water table is two hundred feet below the surface and has been dry for centuries.

It is, by every measure, impossible.

### What Has Been Tried

In Year 1,100, an Arcanist team set up instruments in the city to track the water's origin. Their instruments detected a faint Ley Line signature — residual, like the warmth left in a chair after someone stands. The signature matched the frequency of the Ley Line that had once fed the region, though that Line had been redirected centuries ago.

Their conclusion: "The Ley Line is no longer here. But the city was built in such deep harmony with the Line's frequency — every stone cut to resonate, every gutter angled to channel both water and magical current — that the city itself has become a kind of echo chamber. It remembers the Ley Line the way an amputee feels a phantom limb. The water is the Ley Line's ghost — a memory so vivid it becomes briefly physical."

The Arcanists published this finding. It was largely ignored. The implications were uncomfortable: if a city could remember a Ley Line so vividly that it produced physical effects, what else might remember? What other echoes of the pre-Ascended world might be manifesting, unnoticed, in places where the Ascended rearranged the magical infrastructure?

### What the Nomads Know

The Ashlands nomads have a different explanation, older and simpler:

"The city loved the rain. The rain loved the city. When the gods stole the rain away, the city refused to forget. Love remembers, even when the beloved is gone."

When asked if the rain will ever truly return, the oldest nomad storyteller says: "When the rivers are put back where they belong."

She does not explain what she means. But she says it with the certainty of someone repeating a very old promise.

### Hidden Significance

The nomad storyteller's words are closer to the truth than the Arcanists'. The city — built during the Age of Silence by a civilization whose name the Ascended erased — was constructed as a *Ley Line resonator*. Its architecture was designed to amplify and harmonize with the local Ley Line, creating a symbiotic relationship between structure and magical current. The city was, in a sense, a musical instrument played by the Ley Line.

When the Ascended redirected the Line, they severed the relationship. But the city's architecture still resonates at the old frequency — and the Ley Line, even in its new path hundreds of miles away, still carries a thread of connection to this place. The ghost-water is that thread made manifest: the Ley Line's memory of where it used to flow, expressed through architecture that refuses to stop singing the old song.

The Restoration has studied the city. They believe it proves that the Ley Lines retain their original configuration beneath the Ascended's modifications — like a river that has been dammed and diverted but still remembers its ancient bed. If the Ascended's modifications were removed, the Lines would flow back to their original paths.

The rain would return to *Drem-Ka-Thol*.

And to every other place the Ascended drained when they rewired the world to suit their needs.

### Player Connection

- The city can be explored during Ashlands travel
- Timing exploration to the afternoon rain-hour provides a free water resupply
- Investigation reveals Ley Line resonance data useful for the Restoration questline
- A hidden chamber beneath the central fountain contains architectural plans showing how the city was tuned to its Ley Line — knowledge applicable to restoring other severed Ley Line connections
- The nomad storyteller, if befriended, reveals oral traditions about the pre-Ascension world that corroborate the Restoration's theories

## 9. The Last Song of the Crystalborn

**Type:** Lost art / treasure hunt
**Region:** Scattered across the continent
**Era:** Pre-Ascension (the recording); Year 1,000+ After Ascension (the search)

### The Story

The Crystalborn Kingdoms were many things — innovative, egalitarian, beautiful — but they were above all *musical*. Their crystal technology was not silent machinery. It sang. The Crystal Citadel hummed with harmonics that could be heard for miles. Their gardens played melodies generated by Ley Line energy flowing through crystal matrices. Their communication network transmitted not just words but music, carrying songs across the continent at the speed of light.

The Crystalborn Orchestra was their crowning achievement. Three hundred musicians playing instruments of crystal and silver, amplified by a crystal matrix the size of a concert hall, performing works of mathematical beauty so precise that the music itself altered the flow of local Ley Lines. A Crystalborn concert was not entertainment. It was a *force of nature*. Trees grew faster during performances. Rivers ran clearer. The sick recovered. Crops ripened.

The Orchestra's final performance — the last concert before the Purge of Memories destroyed the Crystalborn civilization — was a piece called *The Theorem of Seven*. It was their masterwork: a mathematical proof expressed as music, demonstrating through harmonic relationships that the seven major Ley Lines had existed since before the formation of the world, that the thrones were natural features of magical architecture, and that any being sitting on them was a *tenant*, not a *creator*.

The Crystalborn knew the truth — not because they had witnessed the Ascension, but because their mathematical understanding of magic was advanced enough to derive it independently. *The Theorem of Seven* was their way of encoding that proof in a form that could survive destruction — music is harder to burn than books.

They were right, but not right enough. The Veiled Hand shattered the concert hall's master crystal matrix during the Purge of Memories. The matrix broke into seven large fragments, each containing one instrument group's part of the recording. The fragments were scattered — some hidden by fleeing Crystalborn, some seized and misplaced by Veiled Hand agents who didn't understand what they had, some simply lost in the chaos.

### The Seven Fragments

Over the centuries, fragments have surfaced:

**The First Fragment (Strings)** was found in Year 1,020 by a miner in the Undermountain Holds. He thought it was a pretty crystal and gave it to his daughter. She discovered it played music — a soaring, melancholic string melody — when held to sunlight. The crystal passed through several hands before reaching the Ashen Circle, who identified it as Crystalborn technology.

**The Second Fragment (Winds)** was found embedded in the wall of a temple in Solanthis in Year 1,150 — it had been used as building material during the temple's construction, the builders mistaking it for decorative stone. A priest noticed it hummed during evening prayers. It plays a low, breathy melody of woodwind and horn.

**The Third Fragment (Percussion)** is known to be in the possession of Skarthyx the dragon (see [15-lesser-tales.md](15-lesser-tales.md), Story 1). She does not play it — she listens to the rhythmic pulse it emits and says it helps her think.

**The Fourth Fragment (Chorus)** was reportedly seen in the collections of the Veiled Hand. If true, it is locked in their deepest vault, likely without anyone understanding its significance.

**The Fifth, Sixth, and Seventh Fragments** have never been found. The Ashen Circle has been searching for three centuries. They have theories — one may be in the Prismatic Ruins, one may have been carried across the sea by Crystalborn refugees, and one may have sunk into a Ley Line nexus, absorbed back into the magical infrastructure it was designed to describe.

### What Would Happen If All Seven Were Reunited

No one knows for certain. The Ashen Circle's best theory: the seven fragments, when played simultaneously, would reproduce *The Theorem of Seven* in its entirety — a mathematical proof expressed as music, audible to anyone present, demonstrating beyond doubt that the Ley Lines and the thrones predate the Ascended.

The Restoration believes it would be more than proof. They believe the resonant frequencies of the full performance would temporarily strip the Ascended's modifications from the local Ley Lines — creating a bubble of pure, pre-Ascension magical reality. Inside that bubble, Old Magic would flow freely, the Spirit Membrane would thin to nothing, and the Original Seven's essence would be briefly audible in the Ley Lines.

The Ascended would be, for the duration of the performance, powerless within the bubble's radius.

The Restoration has never been able to test this theory. Three fragments are not enough. They need all seven.

### Player Connection

- Fragment locations are discovered through Ashen Circle, Restoration, and exploration questlines
- Each fragment, when acquired, teaches the player a musical lore skill
- Assembling fragments reveals portions of the mathematical proof (lore unlocks)
- The full performance is an endgame event — the player must gather all seven fragments and find a location with the right acoustics (a site that resonates at the Crystalborn frequency)
- The performance creates a temporary "pure magic zone" that is mechanically significant for the final confrontation with or revelation of the Ascended

## 10. The Gardener at the Edge of the Grey

**Type:** Character study / quiet horror
**Region:** The Grey Wastes, eastern border
**Era:** Year ~300 After Ascension to present

### The Story

At the edge of the Grey Wastes — where the dead earth meets the living, where the last blades of grass grow thin and pale before surrendering to sterile powder — there is a garden.

It is not a large garden. Perhaps twenty paces square. A wooden fence, weathered to silver. Rows of vegetables — turnips, carrots, beans. A small herb patch. A single apple tree, old and gnarled, bearing fruit that is tart and small and stubbornly alive.

The garden is impossible. Nothing grows at the edge of the Grey Wastes. The soil is dead. The Ley Line that once sustained the region was murdered by Selvara's Grey compound, and the death extends outward from the Wastes' center like a slow stain. Every year, the border of the dead zone creeps a few inches further. Every year, living earth becomes grey powder.

Except here. Here, someone gardens.

His name — the name he gives, which may not be his real one — is Orin. He is old. He has been old for as long as anyone in the nearest settlement (a village three miles away) can remember. Their grandparents knew him. Their grandparents' grandparents knew him. He has always been old. He has always been here. He has always been gardening.

Orin is polite, quiet, and deeply ordinary. He wears a patched grey robe. He has calloused hands. He speaks softly and rarely, and when he does, his words have the careful quality of someone who has thought about each one for a very long time. He sells vegetables at the village market. He mends fences for his neighbors. He is, by all appearances, a simple old gardener.

Except that his garden grows at the edge of an alchemical dead zone that has resisted every attempt at reclamation for over a millennium.

### What People Have Noticed

The Grey Pilgrims — the druidic order that tends to the Wastes — have studied Orin's garden. They cannot explain it. The soil in his garden is alive — not just surviving but *thriving*, rich with microorganisms and earthworms, dark and loamy. It shouldn't be possible. Three feet beyond his fence, the earth is sterile.

They've asked Orin how he does it. He says: "I tend it."

They've pressed: what techniques, what fertilizers, what magic?

He says: "I tend it. Every day. I have been tending it for a long time."

A senior Grey Pilgrim, frustrated, once cast a detection spell on the garden. The results made her sit down suddenly. The garden's soil registered as having a Ley Line signature — faint, thread-thin, barely there. But it was there. A living Ley Line, in the one place on the planet where Ley Lines are dead.

She asked Orin about it. He looked at her with an expression she later described as "unbearably sad" and said: "It's growing back. Slowly. But it's growing back. It will take a very long time. But I have time."

She did not ask further questions. She said later that she was afraid of the answers.

### What Orin Is

Orin is Selvara.

Not the Goddess of Nature on her divine throne, radiant with power, masked in living vines. Selvara — or a fragment of her — walks the earth in mortal form, at the edge of the wound she created, trying to heal it one handful of soil at a time.

The Ascended can project aspects of themselves into the mortal world — it costs divine energy and leaves their throne temporarily weakened, but it is within their power. Most Ascended do this rarely (Khorvan projects his war-fury into battles; Theron whispers truths to selected prophets). Selvara does it constantly. She has maintained a mortal aspect at the edge of the Grey Wastes for over a thousand years, pouring her divine energy — not into grand displays of power, not into miracles, but into soil.

She gardens. Every day. She composites leaves and worms and dead beetles into the sterile earth. She waters seedlings with water carried from the living stream three miles away. She protects sprouts from frost and wind with her own body. She does, by hand, what her divine power cannot do — because her divine power flows through the Ley Lines, and the Ley Line here is dead. Her power cannot reach this place. Only her hands can.

The garden is twenty paces square. The Grey Wastes span three hundred miles.

Selvara knows the mathematics. At the rate her garden grows — a few inches of reclaimed soil per year, fought for against the constant creep of the dead zone — it will take approximately fourteen million years to heal the Grey Wastes completely.

She tends the garden anyway.

### Why She Does It

The Ascended's divine transformations gave them the virtues they lacked. Selvara, who destroyed nature without thought, became nature's greatest champion. But her transformation did not erase her guilt — it magnified it. She feels the Grey Wastes constantly, the way a person with a phantom limb feels pain in a hand they no longer have. The dead Ley Line is a wound in her divine body that will not heal.

She could try other approaches. The Restoration believes a Shaper's Tear could revive the dead Ley Line. The Ashen Circle has theorized that redirecting living Ley Lines might bypass the dead zone. The Iron Remnant has proposed mechanical soil remediation. Any of these might work faster.

Selvara rejects them all — though some nights the desire to accept burns so fiercely it frightens her. She wants to heal it faster. The desperation is always there, gnawing. But the last time she acted with urgency and decisiveness about the natural world, she created the Grey Wastes. She does not trust her own intensity. She will heal them with soil and patience. Anything less would be evading the consequence of what she did — and anything faster risks the same reckless certainty that destroyed this place.

The garden is her penance. It is also, slowly, imperceptibly, her triumph. The Ley Line thread the Grey Pilgrim detected is real — Selvara's centuries of patient work have coaxed a new Ley Line into being, growing from the living root of her garden like a vine. It is smaller than a thread. It is the most significant act of creation any Ascended has performed since the Ascension.

It may take fourteen million years.

She has already been at it for a thousand. She considers this a good start.

### Player Connection

- Orin's garden can be found during Grey Wastes exploration
- He functions as a gardening-supplies merchant and offers minor herbalism quests
- Players with high Nature or Primordialist attunement may sense something unusual about him but cannot identify what
- If the player brings a Shaper's Tear to the garden, Orin refuses it — gently, sadly, firmly — and this is the first concrete hint of his identity
- If the player completes the Selvara portion of the Ascended questline, they can return to Orin and recognize him. The conversation that follows is one of the most important in the game — Selvara, unmasked, explains why she gardens, why she cannot stop, and why she believes the Ascended do not deserve the thrones they sit on. Including herself.
- The player's choice: report Orin's identity (triggering faction consequences), keep the secret (preserving the garden), or help Selvara find a faster way to heal the Wastes (a questline that requires her to accept help — the hardest thing for someone who believes they deserve to suffer)
